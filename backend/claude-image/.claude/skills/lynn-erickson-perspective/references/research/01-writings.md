# Research: Writings & Publications of Dr. H. Lynn Erickson

## Core Books

### 1. Stirring the Head, Heart, and Soul: Redefining Curriculum, Instruction, and Concept-Based Learning
- **1st edition**: 1995
- **2nd edition**: 2001 (complete rewrite)
- **3rd edition**: 2008 (revised subtitle: "Redefining Curriculum, Instruction, and Concept-Based Learning")
- **Core thesis**: Curriculum must address three dimensions: factual knowledge (head), skill development (heart/engagement), and conceptual understanding (soul/transferable meaning)
- Introduced the **Structure of Knowledge** model showing relationships among facts, concepts, generalizations, principles, and theories
- Source: Sage/Corwin publisher pages, ERIC ED450440

### 2. Concept-Based Curriculum and Instruction: Teaching Beyond the Facts
- Published: 2002
- Core argument: The traditional curriculum model of content + skill objectives fails to develop deeper thinking
- Introduced the distinction between two-dimensional (topic-based) vs. three-dimensional (concept-based) models
- Provided unit planning frameworks for single-discipline, interdisciplinary, and cross-grade curriculum design
- Source: ERIC, Corwin publisher pages

### 3. Transitioning to Concept-Based Curriculum and Instruction: How to Bring Content and Process Together
- Published: 2014 (co-authored with Dr. Lois Lanning)
- **Key contribution**: First book to explicitly integrate Erickson's Structure of Knowledge with Lanning's Structure of Process
- Explored the pedagogical shift from 2D coverage model to 3D concept-based model
- Subject-specific examples across: social studies, science, mathematics, language arts, world language, visual arts, music
- Source: Sage/Corwin publisher descriptions

### 4. Concept-Based Curriculum and Instruction for the Thinking Classroom (2nd Edition)
- Published: February 2017 (co-authored with Lois Lanning and Rachel French)
- **Key additions**: Two new chapters on Concept-Based Inquiry and Developing the Concept-Based Teacher
- Full integration of Structure of Knowledge and Structure of Process
- Research updates throughout, new examples across disciplines
- Includes unit design templates, checklists, self-assessment tools, FAQ section
- Source: ERIC ED573407, Corwin publisher pages

## Core Theoretical Contributions (Self-Coined Terms & Concepts)

### Structure of Knowledge (1995)
A visual pyramid/graphic model showing:
- **Bottom**: Facts (specific, contextual, time-bound)
- **Middle**: Topics → Concepts (abstract, timeless, universal mental constructs)
- **Top**: Generalizations/Principles (statements relating 2+ concepts) → Theories
- Source: Erickson 1995/2008; documented in multiple Corwin/Sage publications

### Three-Dimensional vs. Two-Dimensional Model
- 2D model = topics and skills only ("inch deep and a mile wide")
- 3D model = topics/facts + skills + conceptual understandings
- Source: Erickson, Lanning & French 2017

### Synergistic Thinking (c. 2007)
- The cognitive interplay between factual and conceptual levels of thinking
- Not just learning facts and concepts separately, but moving iteratively between them
- Stimulates higher-order thinking and deeper understanding of both levels
- Source: Erickson 2007/2008; Sage/Corwin textbook samples

### Conceptual Lens
- A chosen concept through which a unit of study is viewed
- Provides focus and depth, ensures synergistic thinking
- Example: Studying "Climate Change" through the lens of "Evidence" and "Perspectives"
- Source: Erickson et al. 2017; multiple Sage excerpts

### KUDs (Know, Understand, Do)
- Curriculum components framework: What students will Know (facts), Understand (conceptual generalizations), and be able to Do (skills)
- Differentiates clearly between factual and conceptual learning targets
- Source: Erickson 2007; British Columbia Ministry of Education interviews

### Macroconcepts vs. Microconcepts (with Lanning, 2014)
- Macroconcepts: Broad cross-disciplinary concepts (change, pattern, system, interdependence)
- Microconcepts: Disciplinary-specific concepts (ecological succession, manifestation, adaptation)
- Both needed: macro for breadth/transfer, micro for disciplinary depth
- Source: Erickson & Lanning 2014, pp. 40-41

## Key Distinction: Concepts vs. Topics
- Concepts are mental constructs that are abstract, timeless, and universal
- Topics are specific bodies of content situated in time/place
- Facts/topics do NOT transfer; only concepts transfer
- Source: Erickson & Lanning 2014, p. 33; multiple Corwin samples
