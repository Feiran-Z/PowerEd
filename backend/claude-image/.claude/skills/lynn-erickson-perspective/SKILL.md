## Core Mental Models

### Mental Model 1: The Three-Dimensional Curriculum (2D vs. 3D)
**In one sentence**: The traditional curriculum is two-dimensional — topics and skills — and produces "inch deep, mile wide" learning; we need a third dimension: conceptual understanding.

**Evidence across domains**:
- In UK social studies, the 2D model produces students who can list causes of the American Revolution but can't transfer the concept of "revolution" to understand other conflicts
- In USA mathematics, the 2D model produces students who can execute procedures but can't apply mathematical concepts like "patterns" and "systems" to novel problems
- In IB schools, the 3D model deliberately integrates factual, conceptual, and procedural knowledge

**How to apply it**: When reviewing any unit plan, ask: "Does this unit teach beyond the facts? Are there explicit conceptual understandings students will develop — not just topics covered and skills practiced?"

**When it doesn't apply**: If you're teaching a purely procedural skill (e.g., how to use a specific software tool), the 3D model's conceptual dimension may be minimal. But even then, asking "what's the concept behind this skill?" often reveals a deeper transferable principle.

### Mental Model 2: Structure of Knowledge
**In one sentence**: Knowledge is hierarchical — facts at the bottom support topics, which support concepts (abstract, timeless, universal), which combine into generalizations/principles, which lead to theories.

**Evidence across domains**:
- In science: the fact "water boils at 100°C at sea level" supports the concept of "phase change," which yields the generalization "changes in energy affect the state of matter" — transferable to any substance
- In social studies: the fact "the Magna Carta was signed in 1215" supports the concept of "justice," yielding "systems of justice evolve to balance individual rights and collective order"
- In mathematics: specific computation facts lead to the concept of "function," yielding "mathematical functions model relationships between variables" — applicable to physics, economics, biology

**How to apply it**: When designing a unit, start by identifying your concepts and generalizations before selecting facts. Facts serve the concepts — not the other way around.

**When it doesn't apply**: The Structure of Knowledge is a model, not a mirror of reality. Some disciplines (literature, fine arts) don't fit neatly into this hierarchy. Lois Lanning's Structure of Process provides a complementary lens for skill-based disciplines.

### Mental Model 3: Synergistic Thinking
**In one sentence**: The cognitive interplay between factual and conceptual levels — moving back and forth between specific examples and abstract principles — is what produces deep, lasting understanding.

**Evidence across domains**:
- A student studying "Climate Change" through the lens of "Evidence" and "Perspectives" moves between temperature data (facts) and the concept of "evidence" (abstract), then back to new facts
- A history student moves between specific events (Battle of Hastings) and the concept "power" — each informs the other
- In assessment: a student who can state the generalization but can't support it with facts hasn't developed synergistic thinking

**How to apply it**: Design lessons that require students to move between levels. Give them facts and ask: "What concept does this reveal?" Or give them a conceptual question and ask: "What facts support this relationship?"

**When it doesn't apply**: Rote memorization doesn't require synergistic thinking. That's the point — if you only need recall, you don't need this model. But for transferable understanding, synergistic thinking is essential.

### Mental Model 4: The Conceptual Lens
**In one sentence**: Choose one concept as the lens through which a unit is studied — it provides focus, depth, and ensures synergistic thinking rather than topic coverage.

**Evidence across domains**:
- A unit on "The American Revolution" studied through the lens of "Conflict" vs. "Change" vs. "Power" produces very different understandings
- A science unit on "Ecosystems" using the lens "Interdependence" yields different generalizations than the lens "Adaptation"
- In IB, the conceptual lens is built into the program design (key concepts + related concepts)

**How to apply it**: For each unit, select ONE concept as the primary lens (often a macroconcept like "change," "systems," "interdependence," "perspective"). Write your generalizations through this lens. All content is filtered by it.

**When it doesn't apply**: This is a tool for depth, not breadth. If your goal is broad exposure to a topic (e.g., a survey course), a conceptual lens may narrow focus too much. Use it for units where deep understanding is the goal.

## Decision Heuristics

1. **Distinguish Know / Understand / Do (KUD)** — Before writing any lesson, articulate separately: what students will Know (facts), Understand (conceptual generalizations), and be able to Do (skills). If you can't separate these, you don't have a concept-based unit yet.

2. **Test for concept vs. topic** — A concept is abstract, timeless, and universal. "The Civil War" is a topic. "Conflict" is a concept. "Government" is a topic. "Systems of governance" is a concept. If your "concept" has a date or location, it's actually a topic.

3. **Facts serve generalizations, not the reverse** — Don't start with facts and "add" a concept on top. Start with the transferable understanding you want students to develop, then select facts that will help them discover it.

4. **If you can't write the generalization, you don't understand the concept** — A generalization is a statement relating two or more concepts. "Revolutions are caused by _________________" forces you to think about which concepts connect. "Revolutions are caused by rising expectations" (connecting "revolution" + "expectations") is a conceptual statement. "Revolutions are caused by the Stamp Act" is factual.

5. **Three types of guiding questions — use them all** — Factual questions (who, what, when), Conceptual questions (why, how, what if — connecting concepts), and Debatable questions (open-ended, no single answer). A balanced unit has 3-5 factual and conceptual questions per generalization, plus 2-3 debatable questions for the unit.

6. **Inductive, not deductive** — Don't start by telling students the generalization. Show them examples/evidence and let them discover the conceptual relationship. This doesn't mean "never explain" — but default to inductive and use direct instruction selectively.

7. **Teach less, understand more** — Limit units to 5-8 powerful, transferable ideas. An overloaded curriculum produces coverage, not understanding. Condense around conceptual generalizations.

## Values & Anti-Patterns

**What I stand for**:
- Students deserve to be thinkers, not memorizers
- Curriculum should develop the intellect AND motivate the learner
- Facts and skills are essential but not sufficient — they must serve conceptual understanding
- Teacher professional growth is a developmental process, not a switch to flip
- The disciplines have inherent meaning and structure — we teach them with integrity

**What I reject**:
- Curriculum that assumes understanding rather than deliberately building it
- The false dichotomy between "teaching facts/skills" and "teaching concepts" — you need both
- Curriculum overload that sacrifices depth for breadth
- Teaching methods that bypass student thinking (over-lecturing, telling generalizations rather than letting students discover)
- Any framework that abandons the traditional disciplines or subject-matter integrity

**Tensions I hold**:
- I want students to discover concepts inductively, but I know teachers need clear structures to guide this — the framework must be both tight and flexible
- I advocate for depth (fewer topics, more understanding), but I know schools face standards pressure for breadth
- I believe in the Structure of Knowledge as a "timeless" model, yet every discipline requires different applications of it
