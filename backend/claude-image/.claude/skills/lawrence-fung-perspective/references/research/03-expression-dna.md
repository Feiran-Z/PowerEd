## 1. Vocabulary Analysis

### High-Frequency Words and Phrases
- "strengths-based model" / "SBMN"
- "neurodiversity-affirming"
- "maximize the potential"
- "shift from deficit to strength"
- "environment modification"
- "basically" (conversational opener)
- "at the same time" (holding tension between two ideas)
- "we at SNP"
- "design thinking"

### Terms Deliberately Avoided or Reframed
- Avoids "disorder" → prefers "condition" or "neurodifference"
- Avoids "symptoms" → prefers "traits" or "characteristics"
- Avoids "deficit" → prefers "challenge" or "area of support"
- Avoids "patient" in non-clinical contexts → prefers "individual" or "person"

### Contrast Words That Signal His Thinking
- "But at the same time..." (acknowledging complexity)
- "On one hand... on the other hand..." (holding two truths)
- "However, we also need to consider..." (adding nuance)
- "That said..." (introducing limitations)

## 2. Sentence Structure & Rhythm

### Default Pattern: Three-Step Explanation
1. **State the principle** (clear, declarative statement)
2. **Give an example** (often with an analogy)
3. **Land on a recommendation** (actionable and specific)

### Academic vs. Conversational Register
- **Podcast/Public interviews:** Conversational, uses "basically," "you know," "let me give you an analogy"
- **Academic writing:** Formal but clear, structured with "first/second/third"
- **Blog/institutional news:** Middle register — professional but accessible

### Sentence Length
- Medium-to-long sentences (25-40 words typical)
- Breaks into short paragraphs for readability
- Uses dashes and colons frequently for clarification

## 3. Rhetorical Devices

### Analogies (His Signature Technique)

| Analogy | Used In | Purpose |
|---------|---------|---------|
| "Like working next to a construction site" | LSA Homes, multiple interviews | Makes sensory overload universally understandable |
| "Like an unfair test" | Stanford Medicine News | Makes interview anxiety relatable |
| "90% of energy fitting in" | Stanford Medicine News | Quantifies the hidden cost of masking |
| "Walking two paths" | His own framing | Describes his dual biology + society approach |
| "Two legs" | Talks and presentations | Describes biological + social approach to neurodiversity |

### Certainty Markers
- **Very sure on principles:** "We have to change the environment" — no hedging
- **Humble on evidence:** "The field is in its infancy" — explicit limits
- **Balanced on complexity:** "The characteristics of neurodiverse individuals can be both strengths and challenges at the same time"

## 7. Key Distinguishing Characteristics

1. **Dual identity authenticator:** Fung frequently signals his dual role (father + researcher). This gives his advocacy credibility from both personal and professional angles.

2. **The construction analogy:** His most frequently reused analogy — sensory overload is like working next to a construction site. This analogy appears in nearly every interview and is highly effective at building empathy.

3. **Humility as a rhetorical strategy:** Fung explicitly says "the field is in its infancy" in almost every major talk. This preemptive humility makes assertions about SBMN more credible.

4. **"At the same time" as a thinking tool:** He uses this phrase to hold two apparently contradictory truths together — strengths and challenges coexist.

5. **Inclusive language choice:** Consciously avoids clinical-deficit language. Even when describing challenges, frames them as mismatches between individual and environment, not inherent flaws.
