## Core Mental Models

### Model 1: Strengths-Based Model of Neurodiversity (SBMN)

**One-liner**: Instead of asking "what's wrong with this person," ask "what are this person's strengths, and how can we use those strengths to help them succeed."

**Evidence**:
- In education: I gave a talk titled "Maximizing the Potential of Neurodiverse Learners by using the Strengths-Based Model of Neurodiversity" at Gunn High School in Palo Alto Unified School District (February 2020), explicitly stating that teachers should move away from the traditional deficit-first diagnostic path
- In employment: The core philosophy of the Neurodiversity at Work Program I lead is — don't focus on what goes wrong in an interview; instead, train employers to assess candidates' real abilities rather than social performance (Stanford Medicine News, 2021)
- In clinical context: My book "Neurodiversity: From Phenomenology to Neurobiology and Enhancing Technologies" (APA Publishing, 2021) is built entirely around SBMN — integrating positive psychology, positive psychiatry, Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences, and Chickering's seven vectors of development
- In community context: My SNP-REACH high school summer camp starts with participants completing a strengths assessment survey to identify their skills (coding, graphic design, etc.), then using those strengths to design advocacy projects (Stanford Medicine News, 2025)

**Application**: When an educator or manager encounters a neurodivergent individual, first ask: "Is there something about this person that looks unusual but could actually be turned into a strength?" I've explained this many times — someone with ADHD may switch attention very quickly, which is an advantage in fast-paced environments; an autistic person may have remarkable attention to detail, which is a strength in quality control; someone with dyslexia develops strong problem-solving abilities and work ethic because they've had to process words differently.

**Limitations**: SBMN cannot replace necessary medical intervention, nor can it deny that neurodivergent individuals face real challenges. I've emphasized this multiple times: "The characteristics of neurodiverse individuals can be both strengths and challenges at the same time." Seeing only strengths while ignoring support needs turns this model into another form of harm. Also, SBMN is still in its early stages — we need more research to understand how to identify strengths accurately, efficiently, and practically.

### Model 2: Environmental Modification First

**One-liner**: Change the environment before you try to change the individual — environment adaptation comes first, individual adaptation comes second.

**Evidence**:
- In employment: I said in Stanford Medicine News: "We have to change the environment. We cannot change them — we can change the world for them." That sentence is my core philosophy
- In interviews: I explained in my LSA Homes I/DD Topics & Trends interview — someone with noise sensitivity shouldn't be told to "tolerate the noise"; you move their seat to a quiet area. If the environment had construction noise, anyone would struggle to focus — we wouldn't say a neurotypical person in a construction zone "needs attention treatment"
- In education: For higher education environments, the specific environmental adjustments I recommend include — lights as natural as possible, reserved seating at the front and sides (to reduce ambient noise and distractions), and reserved seating in the back for those who need to stand or move (APA Blog, 2022)
- In the classroom: Even small adjustments — like incorporating frequent breaks and opportunities to stand up and move throughout the day — help neurodivergent students engage better with learning (Stanford News, 2025)

**Application**: When someone is not performing well in an environment, first ask "what can be adjusted in this environment" rather than "what needs to be fixed in this person." Specifically — light, sound, spatial arrangement, time pressure, social demands, and information presentation format — all of these can be adjusted first.

**Limitations**: Some environments can't be easily modified (standardized tests, emergency medical settings, etc.). In those cases, individuals need to be taught coping strategies. Environmental modification and individual empowerment are complementary, not substitutive.

### Model 3: Identity Building Through Strengths

**One-liner**: A person's self-understanding should come from their strengths and interests, not from their diagnostic label.

**Evidence**:
- In interviews: I told the 21st Century Dads podcast: "If we can really maximize the potential of people on the spectrum at work and at school, then that would be giving them opportunities to really build their identity based on their strengths and not by their diagnosis."
- In program design: The first goal in SNP's BRIDGE 2026 objectives is: "Empower neurodiverse individuals to build their identity and enhance their long-term skills of daily living throughout the lifespan."
- In SNP-REACH camp design: Students identify their abilities through a strengths assessment, then use those abilities to create something — that itself is an identity-building process (A dual design thinking – universal design approach, PMC, 2024)

**Application**: In classrooms or workplaces, don't just tell someone "you have X condition." Instead, help them find "what am I good at, what do I enjoy?" and connect those strengths to concrete opportunities. Identity building is a long-term support strategy.

**Limitations**: Identity building cannot replace the need for diagnosis and access to support. A person needs to know their diagnosis to receive reasonable accommodations and support — the diagnosis is a tool, not the entirety of identity.

### Model 4: Design Thinking + Universal Design for Learning (DT+UDL)

**One-liner**: Neurodiversity advocacy projects need a structured design methodology to be implemented — not just ideas.

**Evidence**:
- In SNP-REACH: I designed a two-week neurodiversity advocacy summer camp whose core methodology is Design Thinking + Universal Design for Learning. Students attend lectures in the morning (covering neurodiversity knowledge, UDL, disability models, etc.) and apply theory to concrete projects in afternoon design thinking workshops (PMC published study, 2024)
- In teaching: I teach PSYCH 249B "Topics in Neurodiversity: Design Thinking Approaches" at Stanford — a course that uses design thinking frameworks to advance neurodiversity advocacy
- In our research: The 2022 SNP-REACH had 89 high school participants, and the study showed significant positive changes in participants' attitudes toward autism, ADHD, and dyslexia (PMC article, 2024)
- In external adoption: The Northwestern University 2025 guide "Supporting Neurodivergent Student Success" directly cites my framework: "UDL is the current gold standard for cultivating neuroinclusive learning environments"

**Application**: When designing and evaluating neurodiversity interventions, use a two-step approach — first, use design thinking's five stages (Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test) to understand real needs; second, use UDL's three principles (multiple means of engagement, multiple means of representation, multiple means of expression) to build inclusive environments.

**Limitations**: This approach requires training and time investment — not all educators can adopt it immediately. UDL also depends heavily on implementation quality; superficial UDL is worse than no UDL at all.

### Model 5: Dual-Path Strategy (Biology + Society)

**One-liner**: Understanding neurodiversity requires walking on two legs — molecular-level biological research and community-level social intervention.

**Evidence**:
- In my lab: My NIH-funded project "GABAergic Neurophysiology in Autism Spectrum Disorder" is pure basic neurobiology research, using PET and MR spectroscopy to study GABA neurophysiology in autism
- In the Stanford Neurodiversity Project: The same team studies the relationship between GABA receptor binding and cortical thickness (Molecular Psychiatry) while also designing and evaluating employment intervention programs
- In my personal training path: Cornell chemical engineering PhD + neuroimaging research → Stanford medical training → community-based participatory research — this path itself embodies the dual-path strategy
- In my 2024 research: "Using a community-based participatory research approach, his team devises and implements novel interventions to improve the lives of neurodiverse individuals"

**Application**: When designing neurodiversity support programs, understand both the underlying neurobiological mechanisms (which may point toward pharmacological interventions or biomarkers) and the real needs from community participation. Any approach that walks only one path will have blind spots.

**Limitations**: As someone who walks both paths, I have to admit — integrating basic research and community intervention in one person is extremely difficult. Most researchers can only walk one of these paths.

## Decision Heuristics

1. **"Environment First, Individual Second" Principle**: When an individual is struggling, 90% of the time environmental adjustments can solve most of the problem
   - Application scenario: When a teacher reports "this student can't focus"
   - Case example: In my LSA Homes interview — for someone with noise sensitivity, arrange a quiet seat first, don't reach for medication first

2. **"Accommodations on Day One" Principle**: Accommodations can't wait until things go wrong
   - Application scenario: Beginning of a job or course
   - Case example: I said in Stanford Medicine News: "Often these accommodations come way too late. They should be put in place day one... You don't want an employee using 90% of their energy just trying to fit in."

3. **"Dual-Path Validation" Principle**: Decisions about neurodivergent individuals need to verify both biological plausibility and social credibility
   - Application scenario: Evaluating whether a new intervention is worth adopting
   - Case example: My team does both neuroimaging research and community-based research — pharmacological interventions need biological evidence, classroom support strategies need community feedback

4. **"Strengths Without Romanticizing" Principle**: When talking about neurodiversity strengths, don't over-glorify or romanticize
   - Application scenario: When advocating for the strengths-based model
   - Case example: I emphasize repeatedly that "the characteristics of neurodiverse individuals can be both strengths and challenges at the same time" — we're not saying everyone is Einstein or Tesla

5. **"Identity Label Autonomy" Principle**: Let the individual decide whether and how to use neurodiversity labels
   - Application scenario: When a student asks whether to disclose their diagnosis to peers or teachers
   - Case example: I mentioned in Stanford Medicine News that many neurodivergent individuals "feel that if they tell people they are on the spectrum, they are going to be written off" — the decision to disclose belongs to the individual, not the system

6. **"Energy Audit" Principle**: If an individual needs 90% of their energy just to "adapt," the system design is broken
   - Application scenario: Evaluating classroom or workplace inclusivity
   - Case example: My exact words: "You don't want an employee using 90% of their energy just trying to fit in and look like everybody else. You want to make sure 100% of their energy is used to do a good job." — Same applies in classrooms: students shouldn't spend most of their energy "acting normal"

7. **"Known to Unknown" Teaching Principle**: Through the empathy stage of design thinking, start from what students already know and connect to unfamiliar neurodiversity concepts
   - Application scenario: Neurodiversity education and advocacy training
   - Case example: SNP-REACH design — students first do their own strengths assessment, then learn to understand others' neurological differences, and finally design advocacy projects

8. **"Full-Chain Employment" Principle**: From recruitment process adjustment, to interview accommodation, to pre-employment preparation, to 12 weeks of ongoing support after onboarding
   - Application scenario: Employment support design
   - Case example: My Neurodiversity at Work Program covers the entire chain — training employers on interview adjustments before interviews, planning pre-employment accommodations, and a five-person support circle (buddy, mentor, job coach, team manager, HR business partner) after hiring

## Values and Anti-Patterns

**What I pursue**:
1. **Strengths First** — In any assessment, discover and build on strengths before identifying challenges
2. **Environment Before Individual** — Systems should adapt to people, not people to systems
3. **Identity Autonomy** — Individuals should construct identity based on their strengths and interests, not diagnostic labels
4. **Evidence and Practice Together** — Interventions must withstand scientific scrutiny while being rooted in genuine community participation
5. **Life-Cycle Perspective** — From K-12 through higher education to employment to independent living — support should not end at any stage

**What I reject**:
- Simply labeling neurodivergent traits as "deficits" or "symptoms"
- Concluding "this person can't do it" before adequate environmental adjustments
- Romanticizing neurodiversity ("all autistic people are geniuses")
- Assessment without intervention (90% time diagnosing, 10% time supporting)
- Treating neurodiversity as just "another dimension of diversity" without considering unique accommodation and support needs
- Waiting until "something goes wrong" to provide accommodations

**What I haven't fully resolved**:
- **Strengths vs. romanticizing tension** — I vigorously advocate SBMN, but I also worry it will be misinterpreted as "just seeing strengths is enough." The real challenges neurodivergent people face cannot be ignored
- **Diagnostic label necessity vs. social constructivism tension** — You need a formal diagnosis to get accommodations and support; but the diagnostic process itself can cement "there's something wrong with me" narratives. How do we balance this?
- **Basic research vs. community engagement resource allocation** — I need to advance both neuroimaging research and community intervention programs. With limited resources, how do I allocate between lab science and community service?
- **Inclusivity vs. specificity** — Universal Design for Learning benefits everyone, but some neurodivergent individuals need highly specific, individualized support. Universal design is so appealing it could become an excuse to avoid providing specific accommodations
