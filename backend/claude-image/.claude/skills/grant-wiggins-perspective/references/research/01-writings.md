# Grant Wiggins -- Complete Writings Research

## Overview

Grant Wiggins (1950-2015) was an American educational consultant, assessment expert, and co-creator of the Understanding by Design (UbD) framework with Jay McTighe. He was president of Authentic Education, previously director of the Center on Learning, Assessment, and School Structure (CLASS), and worked with Ted Sizer's Coalition of Essential Schools. He earned his EdD from Harvard University and his BA from St. John's College in Annapolis.

## SECTION 1: BOOKS

### 1.1 Understanding by Design (1998, Expanded 2nd Edition 2005)

**Authors:** Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe
**Publisher:** ASCD
**ISBN (2nd ed):** 1416600353 / 9781416600350
**Pages:** 370 (2nd ed), 201 (1st ed)
**URL:** https://www.ascd.org/books/understanding-by-design-expanded-2nd-edition

**Core Thesis:** What is understanding and how does it differ from knowledge? The book proposes a framework for designing curriculum "backward" from desired results.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Backward Design (3 Stages):**
   - Stage 1: Identify Desired Results -- What should students know, understand, and be able to do? What enduring understandings are desired? What essential questions will be explored?
   - Stage 2: Determine Acceptable Evidence -- How will we know if students have achieved the desired results? What performance tasks reveal understanding?
   - Stage 3: Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction -- What activities, sequence, and resources will achieve the goals?

2. **The Six Facets of Understanding:**
   - **Explanation** -- Can explain concepts, principles, processes in own words; justify answers; show reasoning
   - **Interpretation** -- Can make sense of data, text, experience through images, analogies, stories, models
   - **Application** -- Can effectively use and adapt knowledge in new and complex contexts
   - **Perspective** -- Can see the big picture; recognize different points of view; critique
   - **Empathy** -- Can perceive sensitively; "walk in someone else's shoes"
   - **Self-Knowledge** -- Shows metacognitive awareness; reflects on meaning of learning; recognizes own biases

3. **The UbD Template** -- A structured planning tool embodying the three stages, updated across editions (Version 2.0 in 2011)

4. **Big Ideas and Essential Questions** -- Curriculum organized around transferable concepts and provocative questions

5. **Three Nested Circles of Priority:**
   - Outer: Content worth being familiar with
   - Middle: Important knowledge and skills
   - Inner (core): Big ideas and core tasks (enduring understandings)

6. **WHERETO Acronym** for Stage 3 planning:
   - W -- Where and Why (help students know where they're headed and why)
   - H -- Hook (engage through thought-provoking experiences)
   - E -- Explore/Experience/Equip
   - R -- Reflect/Rethink/Revise
   - E -- Exhibit/Evaluate
   - T -- Tailor (differentiate)
   - O -- Organize (sequence for optimal learning)

**Chapter Outline (2nd Edition):**
1. Backward Design
2. Understanding Understanding
3. Gaining Clarity on Our Goals
4. The Six Facets of Understanding
5. Essential Questions: Doorways to Understanding
6. Crafting Understandings
7. Thinking Like an Assessor
8. Criteria and Validity
9. Planning for Learning
10. Teaching for Understanding
11. The Design Process
12. The Big Picture: UbD as Curriculum Framework
13. "Yes, but..."

**Practical Advice for Unit Planning:**
- Start with goals, not activities or textbooks
- "Think like an assessor" before designing instruction
- Distinguish between acquisition, meaning-making, and transfer (A-M-T)
- Use GRASPS (Goal, Role, Audience, Situation, Product/Performance, Standards) for performance task design
- Design Standards guide self-assessment and peer review

### 1.4 Essential Questions: Opening Doors to Student Understanding (2013)

**Authors:** Jay McTighe and Grant Wiggins
**Publisher:** ASCD
**ISBN:** 9781416615057 / 1416615709
**Pages:** 119
**URL:** https://www.ascd.org/books/essential-questions

**Purpose:** Practical guidance on designing, initiating, and embedding inquiry-based teaching through essential questions.

**Core Content:**
- Comprehensive explanation of why EQs are important
- Seven defining characteristics of EQs
- Distinction between topical and overarching questions
- How to create effective EQs from standards, understandings, and student misconceptions
- Response strategies for student engagement
- Creating a culture of inquiry

**Seven Characteristics of Essential Questions:**
1. Cause genuine and relevant inquiry into big ideas and core content
2. Provoke deep thought, lively discussion, sustained inquiry, and new understanding
3. Require students to consider alternatives, weigh evidence, support ideas, justify answers
4. Stimulate vital, ongoing rethinking of big ideas, assumptions, prior lessons
5. Spark meaningful connections with prior learning and personal experiences
6. Naturally recur, creating opportunities for transfer to other situations
7. Are open-ended -- no single "right" answer

**Practical Advice:**
- Use a checklist for self- or peer-assessment of draft EQs
- Distinguish between "essential to me as a teacher" vs. "essential to anyone as a thinking person"
- EQs should be spiral-like -- returned to repeatedly across a unit/course
- Implement through a four-phase process: Introduce, Elicit responses, Explore new perspectives, Connect to larger understandings

### 1.5 Schooling by Design: Mission, Action, and Achievement (2007)

**Authors:** Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe
**Publisher:** ASCD
**ISBN:** 9781416605805 / 1416605800
**Pages:** 287
**URL:** https://www.ascd.org/books/schooling-by-design

**Purpose:** Extends UbD principles from individual units to whole-school reform.

**Six Pillars of Reform:**
1. Relentless focus on long-term mission: enabling learners to demonstrate understanding and mature habits of mind
2. Curriculum and assessment framework that honors the mission, ending "coverage" as the accepted approach
3. Principles of learning supporting all decisions about pedagogy and planning
4. Structures, policies, job descriptions, practices, and resource use consistent with mission
5. Overall strategy including ongoing feedback and adjustment
6. Tactics linked to strategy, including backward design for key reform work

**Chapter Outline:**
1. What Is the Mission of Schooling?
2. What Should Curriculum Accomplish?
3. How Should Curriculum Be Re-Formed?
4. What Is the Teacher's Job?
5. What Is the Leader's Role?
6. How Should Teaching Be Appropriately Depersonalized?
7. What Is the Overarching Goal?
8. What Habits Must We Confront?
9. How Should Backward Design Apply to School Reform?
10. What Are the Desired Results of School Reform?
11. What Evidence Should We Collect?
12. What Actions Should We Plan?

**Key Idea:** Schools must design themselves backward from their mission, just as teachers design units backward from desired results. Addresses the gap between stated goals and actual practices.

### 1.8 Assessing Student Performance: Exploring the Purpose and Limits of Testing (1993)

**Author:** Grant P. Wiggins (solo)
**Publisher:** Jossey-Bass (Jossey-Bass Education Series)
**ISBN:** 9781555425920 (hardcover), 9780787950477 (paperback)
**Pages:** 316
**ERIC:** ED415248
**URL:** https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Assessing+Student+Performance%3A+Exploring+the+Purpose+and+Limits+of+Testing-p-9780787950477

**Core Thesis:** Student assessment should improve performance, not just audit it. Testing should be only a small facet of assessment.

**Chapter Outline:**
1. Introduction: Assessment and the Morality of Testing
2. Assessment Worthy of the Liberal Arts
3. The Morality of Test Security
4. Testing and Tact
5. Incentives and Assessment
6. Feedback
7. Authenticity, Context, and Validity
8. Accountability: Standards, Not Standardization

**Key Ideas:**
- Assessment is more than testing; intellectual performance is more than right answers
- Critiques problematic practices: test secrecy, distracters, scoring on a curve, formats preventing explanation
- Feedback is an absolute requirement of any authentic test
- More testing cannot provide better accountability; standards, not standardization, are needed
- Traditional validation methods are inadequate for authentic assessment

## SECTION 2: KEY ARTICLES AND PAPERS

### 2.1 ASCD Educational Leadership Articles

**"Teaching to the (Authentic Test)"** (April 1989)
- *Educational Leadership*, 46(7), 41-47
- ERIC: EJ387143
- **Key Ideas:** First print use of "authentic assessment" phrase. Tests should be instructional, not just measurement devices. Schools need to rethink diploma requirements and grading systems.

**"A True Test: Toward More Authentic and Equitable Assessment"** (May 1989)
- *Phi Delta Kappan*, 70(9), 703-713
- ERIC: EJ388723
- **Key Ideas:** Landmark article. Presenting the argument that true tests require performance of exemplary tasks. Authentic assessments replicate real-world challenges. Critiques standardized testing as analogous to mistaking pulse rate for total health.

**"Standards, Not Standardization: Evoking Quality Student Work"** (February 1991)
- *Educational Leadership*, 48(5), 18-25
- ERIC: EJ421344
- **Key Ideas:** Schools need genuine standards (consistent, high expectations) not standardization (uniform testing). Faculties must judge local work against authentic standards and benchmarks.

**"Creating Tests Worth Taking"** (May 1992)
- *Educational Leadership*, 49(8)
- **Key Ideas:** Design criteria for performance assessments. Tests should be "enticing" -- authentic simulations of adult work. Assessment reform as the Trojan horse of real school reform.

**"Practicing What We Preach in Designing Authentic Assessments"** (December 1996/January 1997)
- *Educational Leadership*, 54(4)
- URL: https://www.ascd.org/el/articles/practicing-what-we-preach-in-designing-authentic-assessments

**"Put Understanding First"** (May 2008) -- with Jay McTighe
- *Educational Leadership*, 65(8), 36-41
- URL: https://www.ascd.org/el/articles/put-understanding-first
- **Key Ideas:** The mission of high school is not to cover content but to help learners become thoughtful about and productive with content. Three interrelated goals: acquire, make meaning, transfer. Rethinking instructional sequence.

**"Examining the Teaching Life"** (March 2010) -- with Jay McTighe
- *Educational Leadership*, 67(6)
- URL: https://www.ascd.org/el/articles/examining-the-teaching-life

- URL: https://www.ascd.org/el/articles/how-to-make-your-questions-essential

**"A Diploma Worth Having"** (March 2014)
- *Educational Leadership*, 71(6)
- URL: https://www.ascd.org/el/articles/a-diploma-worth-having

- *Educational Leadership*

**"You Can Teach for Meaning"** (April 2010) -- with Jay McTighe

- *Educational Leadership*

## SECTION 3: SELF-ASSESSMENT RUBRICS AND CURRICULUM REVIEW TOOLS

### 3.1 UbD Design Standards

From *Understanding by Design* (2005) and the UbD White Paper (2012):

**Stage 1 Design Standards:**
- Transfer goals specify genuine, real-world accomplishment
- Understandings reflect important, transferable ideas
- Understandings stated as full-sentence generalizations ("Students will understand that...")
- Essential questions are open-ended and thought-provoking
- Standards, mission, or program goals addressed explicitly in all three stages
- Knowledge and skill support established goals, understandings, and transfer
- All Stage 1 elements are aligned and coherent

**Stage 2 Design Standards:**
- Assessments provide valid evidence of all desired results (alignment with Stage 1)
- Assessments include authentic transfer tasks based on one or more facets of understanding
- Assessments provide sufficient information to make valid inferences
- Evaluative criteria for each assessment are aligned to desired results

**Stage 3 Design Standards:**
- WHERETO elements included so unit is engaging and effective for all learners
- Learning events aligned with Stage 1 goals and Stage 2 assessments

### 3.2 Six-Facet Rubric

From *Understanding by Design* (2005), Figure 8.3:
- Explanation: accurate, systematic, justified
- Interpretation: meaningful, insightful, illuminating
- Application: effective, apt, fluent, adaptive
- Perspective: credible, broad, critical
- Empathy: sensitive, open, receptive
- Self-Knowledge: self-aware, reflective, metacognitive

### 3.3 GRASPS Framework for Performance Task Design

- **G** -- Goal (task goal/challenge)
- **R** -- Role (student's role)
- **A** -- Audience (target audience)
- **S** -- Situation (context/scenario)
- **P** -- Product/Performance (what students produce)
- **S** -- Standards (criteria for success)

### 3.4 WHERETO Self-Assessment

- **W** -- Where/Why (help students know where headed, why)
- **H** -- Hook (engage interest)
- **E** -- Explore/Experience/Equip (active learning, instruction)
- **R** -- Reflect/Rethink/Revise (deepen understanding)
- **E** -- Evaluate (exhibit, demonstrate)
- **T** -- Tailor (differentiate)
- **O** -- Organize (sequence for optimal learning)

### 3.5 Peer Review Process

From "Practicing What We Preach in Designing Authentic Assessments" (1996) and the UbD Handbook:

- Clear design standards for products
- Design template, process, and self-assessment/peer review based on standards
- Review criteria for self- and peer-assessment
- Rubrics for evaluating curriculum designs
- Standards-based work (not process-based or good-intentions-based)
- Peer review required for major assessments

### 3.6 Observable Indicators of Teaching for Understanding

From McTighe, Seif, and Wiggins (2000):

**Unit/Course Design Indicators:**
- Reflects coherent design -- big ideas and essential questions guide and are aligned with assessments and instruction
- Incorporates instruction and assessment reflecting the six facets
- Anchors assessment with authentic performance tasks
- Uses clear criteria and performance standards for evaluations
- Students engaged in activities promoting explanation, interpretation, application, perspective, empathy, self-assessment
- Students involved in self- or peer-assessment based on established criteria
- Students use criteria/rubrics to guide and revise work
- Big ideas, essential questions, scoring rubrics are posted

### 3.7 Four Types of Evaluative Criteria for Assessments

From the UbD Advanced Guide (2012):
- **Impact** -- effectiveness of product at reaching goal
- **Content** -- accuracy and completeness
- **Quality** -- excellence of work
- **Process** -- appropriateness of design for the task
