# Colin Beard: Decisions & Actions

## Career-Level Decisions

### 1. From Zoologist to Experiential Learning Theorist

**Context:** Originally trained as a zoologist (BSc, University of London, 1975). Led a 3-month Amazon rainforest zoological expedition at age 22. Worked as: RSPB warden in mountain areas, conservation director of a UK Wildlife Trust, director of a practical charity, managing nature reserves, protecting land.

**Decision:** Transitioned from conservation/nature management into experiential learning — first through outdoor/adventure education, then into higher education pedagogy.

**Significance:** This is not a typical academic path. Beard brings an ecologist's mindset to learning design: systems thinking, ecological relationships, environment-organism interaction. The "fish don't know they're in water" metaphor comes from a real ecologist's perspective.

### 2. PhD Late in Career (completed 2008)

**Context:** While already established as a National Teaching Fellow (2005) and co-authoring the first edition of *Experiential Learning* (2002), Beard pursued a PhD at Sheffield Hallam University.

**Decision:** Formalised his practitioner experience into academic research. The PhD title: "Experiential Learning: The Development of a Pedagogic Framework for Effective Practice."

**Significance:** This gave theoretical legitimacy to his practical work. The PhD was "mode two research" — appropriate to the broad, multi-disciplinary nature of experiential learning. He consciously chose a research methodology that bridged practice and theory.

### 3. Building "Experience — The Difference" Consultancy Alongside Academia

**Context:** While a full-time Professor at Sheffield Hallam University, Beard also ran a private consultancy.

**Decision:** Maintain both academic position AND commercial practice simultaneously.

**Significance:** This dual identity shapes his entire output. His work is tested in real corporate and government settings, not just in academic journals. Clients include: US Foreign Service (diplomat training), Singapore Ministry of Education, global corporates. This gives his models a practicality rare in academic work.

### 4. Global Focus (Not UK-Centric)

**Context:** Most UK academics focus on UK institutions. Beard's work spans Singapore, Malaysia, China, Hong Kong, India, Chile, Peru, Europe, US, Taiwan.

**Decision:** Invested heavily in international relationships — visiting professorships in China (two universities), Hong Kong, Derby; Master Classes across Asia; keynotes on 6 continents.

**Significance:** This global exposure means his models have been tested across cultures. His understanding of "experience" is not Western-centric. The 2026 book comes from 40+ years of global practice.

### 5. Co-founding the Experiential Learning Design Institute (ELDI)

**Context:** Recent development. ELDI is "the world's premier international certification institute for experiential learning design," based in Hong Kong.

**Decision:** Institutionalise his framework beyond his own teaching — create a certification pathway.

**Significance:** This moves Beard from individual practitioner to founder of a school/institution. It also suggests a succession plan — his models will outlive his direct teaching. ELDI offers Level 1-3 certification programmes.

## Intellectual Decisions (Framework Choices)

### 6. Rejecting the Learning Cycle
Beard's most consequential intellectual decision: explicitly position his work AGAINST the dominant "plan, do, review" / Kolb cycle paradigm. This is a deliberate break from the mainstream of experiential learning practice.

### 7. Choosing the Learning Combination Lock Metaphor
Instead of a cycle (which implies sequence and repetition), Beard chose a combination lock (which implies multiple dials that must all align). This design decision reflects: complexity > simplicity, simultaneous variables > sequential stages, multiple right combinations > one right way.

### 8. From 6 Dimensions to 7 Modes (HELM)
The move from the 6-dimension model (Belonging, Doing, Sensing, Feeling, Knowing, Being) in the 2010 Toolkit to the 7-mode HELM (adding Becoming, reconfiguring relationships) in the 2022 book. Beard does not treat his own models as fixed — he evolves them.

### 9. The LED Acronym (2018)
Choosing to co-opt "LED" — a widely recognised technology acronym — as a metaphor for learning design. This is a deliberate marketing/communication decision: replace an old, inefficient model (incandescent = content-lecture) with a new efficient one (LED = experience design).

## Behavioural Patterns

### How He Makes Decisions
- **Iterative, not revolutionary:** Builds each model on previous ones; doesn't discard, extends
- **Evidence-practice oscillation:** Moves between real-world testing and theoretical refinement
- **Collaborative:** John Wilson is his primary co-author (all editions of *Experiential Learning*)
- **Risk-tolerant:** Left zoology/conservation for experiential learning when it was not a recognised field; launched consultancy alongside academia; operates globally rather than locally

### Consistency Check
- **What he says vs what he does:** Consistent — his books model experiential approaches; his Master Classes are experiential; ELDI certifies others to do the same
- **Has he changed his mind?** Yes — the evolution from Learning Combination Lock to HELM shows willingness to update. But the core conviction (experience-centred, whole-person, multi-dimensional) is remarkably consistent across 20+ years
- **Contradictions:** The model proliferation (LCL, 6D, LED, HELM, 5H) could be seen as contradictory or as cumulative. Beard himself seems to treat them as compatible at different levels of analysis
