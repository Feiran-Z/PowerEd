# Anne Meyer: Conversations & Spontaneous Thinking

## Source 1: Smithsonian Oral History Interview (May 4, 1994)

**Source:** https://americanhistory.si.edu/comphist/annemeyr.html (first-hand, highest)

Key moments revealing her thinking:

- **Her frustration-driven origin**: "We felt that writing a report and trying to suggest maybe a different book or suggest that kids be in smaller classroom or things like that, that ultimately we didn't feel we were having the impact we would like to have." — This dissatisfaction with clinical work is what launched CAST.

- **The "Matthew moment"**: When she digitized Matthew's transformers book and he could read it independently for the first time: "He sat and he read this book from beginning to end. He was rigid with excitement. Sweat was pouring off of him and he was just beside himself because he could actually do this."

- **Paradigm shift explained in real-time**: "Initially we saw that the issues we were dealing with as somehow residing in the kids... We really see it completely different now. We see the problem as residing in the curriculum itself."

- **Views on print**: "I'm one of the great fans of print. I'm not going to curl up with my computer at night and read unless it were the only way I could do it." — Not anti-print, but pro-flexibility.

- **On resistance to change**: "You have to think about how you can bring about change in a way that's constructive and is really going to be effective." — Pragmatic, not revolutionary.

- **On organizational culture**: When the hospital told them to fire someone, "we all cut back by a small amount in terms of what we were paid and we kept everybody on. That was more of our mentality. We're very collaborative."

- **Civil rights framing**: "Just as its recognized as a violation of civil rights to have only stairs as an access route to a building... we'd like it to be considered a violation of civil rights to have only print as a means of delivering curriculum."

- **On standard interfaces being inaccessible**: "None of standard interfaces are accessible right now." (1994, about the information highway) — Foresight about digital accessibility.

## Source 2: Harvard Ed Magazine Interview (2014)

**Source:** https://www.gse.harvard.edu/ideas/ed-magazine/14/01/all-along

- **Key quote**: "Teachers used to think, 'Oh, I have this problem kid.' We wanted to change the thinking to, 'How do we make this curriculum so that it helps whoever walks in the door?'" — The essence of UDL in one sentence.

- **On CAST's future**: "We want to see education changed. We want to see this approach be the norm, we want these tools available to everyone. We want to see UDL as a reform initiative, one that we hope will really take hold nationwide and worldwide."

- **On David Rose**: Noted that Rose "has always wanted to surround himself with people who are smarter than himself and distribute the work."

## Spontaneous Thinking Patterns

- **Analogies**: She consistently uses architectural analogies (curb cuts, ramps) to explain educational design — showing her cross-domain thinking from design background.
- **Concrete-first**: In the oral history, every abstract principle is anchored to a specific child (Matthew, Megan).
- **Collaborative framing**: Almost never uses "I" for CAST's achievements — always "we." Even in describing her own educational background.
- **Honest about limits**: "We haven't been able to create a wonderful tool for him to draw with yet" — candid about remaining challenges.
- **Policy-aware but practitioner-focused**: She moves fluidly between talking about individual kids and systemic policy changes.
