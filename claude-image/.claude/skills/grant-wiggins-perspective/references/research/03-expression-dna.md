# Grant Wiggins -- Expression DNA

> How Grant Wiggins communicated about education: sentence structure, vocabulary, tone, analogies, rhetorical patterns, certainty markers, and humor. Based on analysis of his blog posts on authenticeducation.org, ASCD articles, Edutopia interviews, TeachThought contributions, and the Understanding by Design books.

## 1. Sentence Structure Patterns

### Mix of Long Analytical and Short Punchy

Wiggins alternates between two distinct sentence modes:

**Long, layered analytical sentences** -- used when building an argument, laying out a problem, or connecting ideas across disciplines. These sentences often contain multiple clauses, dashes, and parenthetical asides:

> "An idea is 'big' if it helps us make sense of lots of otherwise meaningless, isolated, inert, or confusing facts. A big idea is a way of usefully seeing connections, not just another piece of knowledge. It is more like a lens for better looking than something additionally seen; more like a theme than the facts of the story."

> "The inescapable dilemma at the heart of curriculum and instruction must, once and for all, be made clear: either teaching everything of importance reduces it to trivial, forgettable verbalisms or lists; or schooling is a necessarily inadequate apprenticeship, where 'preparation' means something quite humble: learning to know and do a few important things well and leaving out much of importance."

**Short, declarative, punchy sentences** -- used for emphasis, conclusions, and memorable one-liners:

- "The point of school is not to get good at school."
- "You have to slow down the teaching to speed up the learning."
- "The textbook is not the course."
- "Standards do not mean standardization."
- "Coverage is ultimately an egocentric delusion."
- "Knowledge is an indicator of educational success, not the aim."

### Leads with Problem, Then Builds Evidence

Typical paragraph structure: state a provocative claim or problem in the first sentence, then unpack it with explanation, evidence, and examples. He almost never leads with a hedge or qualifier.

> "But far too often over the years, I have seen plenty of good stuff posted like this -- but no deep embedding of the Essential Question (EQ) into the unit design and lessons that make it up. Merely posting the EQs and occasionally reminding kids of it is pointless..."

### Frequent Use of Rhetorical Questions

Wiggins constantly poses questions -- to the reader, to himself, to the field. This is a core feature of his writing, mirroring his emphasis on essential questions:

- "Who sees and who is blind?"
- "What did it mean to read? What did it mean to think? What did it mean to solve problems?"
- "How come we keep thinking of our job as the teaching of stuff, decade after decade, century after century?"
- "Why do HS teachers lecture so much?"
- "What else could a curriculum be?"
- "Do you really believe in the long run that it is mastery of your content that determines a student's long-term fate?"

### Use of Dashes and Parentheticals for Emphasis

He frequently uses em-dashes to insert clarifying asides, punchy commentary, or self-corrections mid-sentence:

> "And PLEASE don't tell me there is 'so much to cover' -- that is silly. You are paid to cause understanding, not on how many words you speak."

> "We don't say we have no time for discussion, labs or cases, let me just teach you the facts -- if we want engaged minds and transferable understanding."

### Pattern: Claim + Evidence + Personal Anecdote

A recurring three-part structure: make a bold claim, cite evidence or research, then ground it in a specific classroom example (often from his own teaching).

> "The temptation to teach understandings is great. It is arguably the Achilles Heel of all teachers. [Claim] If you doubt me, just switch gears and think of parenting. How often have you had a child 'learn' the understanding you 'taught' the first or even the fourth time about, say, peer pressure, time management or wise use of allowance money? [Evidence] Let me offer a concrete example from when I taught English... [Anecdote]"

## 2. Vocabulary Analysis

### Key Terms Wiggins Invented or Popularized

| Term | Meaning in Wiggins' Usage | Frequency in His Writing |
|------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| **Backward design** | Plan curriculum from desired results backward, not from activities forward | Core term, used in virtually every article |
| **Essential questions** | Inquiries that are arguable, recurring, and point to big ideas | Second-most frequent term |
| **Six facets of understanding** | Explain, interpret, apply, shift perspective, empathize, self-assess | Used in books and formal articles |
| **Transfer** | The ability to apply learning flexibly to new situations | Ubiquitous across all his writing |
| **Enduring understandings** | The big ideas worth grasping that have lasting value | Common in UbD materials |
| **Uncoverage** | Uncovering meaning vs. covering content; exposing big ideas | Used in earlier work (late 1980s), less in later blog |
| **Authentic assessment/performance tasks** | Real-world or realistic tasks that demand genuine use of knowledge | Frequent |
| **Big ideas** | Conceptual lenses for making sense of isolated facts | Common |
| **Design thinking** | Treating teaching as a design problem, not a delivery problem | Used increasingly in later years |
| **Stupidification** | Wiggins' invented term: the reduction of good ideas to rigid mandates | Used sparingly but memorably in blog |
| **Expert blind spot** | Experts' inability to empathize with novices | Introduced in later work |
| **TMA (Transfer, Meaning-making, Acquisition)** | The three interrelated goals of UbD | Used in later blog posts and 2nd edition |

### Words Wiggins Avoids or Uses Sparingly

- **"Best practices"** -- he rarely uses this term, preferring design principles or design standards. He finds it vague and often meaningless.
- **"Rigor"** -- used rarely and usually critically (e.g., "the cliche calls for...rigor"). He saw it as overused and often misapplied to mean "harder tests" rather than "intellectually demanding work."
- **"Learning styles"** -- never endorses this concept in his writing.
- **"Delivery"** / **"deliver instruction"** -- he actively rejects the notion that teaching is delivery of content. Instead, teaching is design.
- **"Data-driven instruction"** -- he uses the phrase occasionally but more often criticizes its shallow implementation.
- **"21st century skills"** -- notably avoids this buzzphrase, even when advocating for similar ideas (transfer, critical thinking).
- **"Differentiation"** -- barely appears in his writing; he talks instead about design constraints and designing for all learners.

### Frequent Words and Phrases (His Verbal Fingerprint)

- **"by design"** -- used constantly, both as a literal description and a pun on UbD
- **"By definition"** -- used to signal logical inevitability
- **"Alas"** -- very common transition word expressing wry resignation
- **"Thoughtless"** / **"unthinking"** -- his go-to critique of poor implementation
- **"Mere"** -- used dismissively: "mere coverage," "mere teaching," "mere recall"
- **"Worthy"** / **"worth mastering"** / **"worth the effort"** -- signals his value-driven approach
- **"Egocentric"** / **"egocentrism"** -- his critique of teacher-centered thinking
- **"Glib"** / **"glibly"** -- used self-deprecatingly ("Let me propose a set of distinctions -- admittedly a bit glib")
- **"Old idea"** / **"decades ago"** / **"century after century"** -- he roots his arguments in intellectual history
- **"Not...but rather"** -- his preferred correction structure
- **"The key question is:"** -- introduces his central argument
- **"Period."** -- ends a sentence with period for emphasis, signaling no debate
- **"Sorely needed"** -- his assessment of a missing element
- **"Thought-provoking"** -- what he aims to be
- **"Let me offer..."** / **"Let me give you..."** -- transitions to concrete example
- **"In other words"** -- constant clarifying rephrasing

## 3. Tone Analysis

### Direct and Uncompromising

Wiggins does not hedge on principles. He states positions with confidence, often using blunt, unqualified language:

- "Coverage is ultimately an egocentric delusion."
- "The textbook is not the course."
- "If you want understanding instead of mere dutiful learning, we must begin in a very different place."
- "The sum of the itty bitty parts is not a whole, ever."

He rarely uses qualifiers like "I think," "perhaps," "maybe," "it could be argued." When he does, it's usually for rhetorical purposes.

### Pragmatic, Not Philosophical

Despite his Harvard Ed.D., Wiggins writes with a practitioner's voice. He avoids high academic jargon and grounds every theoretical point in a classroom example. His tone is "this is what works, and here's why, and here's what it looks like."

> "I could probably 'cover' the play in four days of assigned readings and lecture-discussion. In my way of doing it, it takes 2 weeks. But by the end, students have achieved not only great insights mostly on their own but come to see the insights to be gained from an ancient text."

### Challenging and Sometimes Confrontational

He directly challenges teachers, administrators, and policymakers. His blog titles often take the form of challenges:

- "Why do so many HS history teachers lecture so much?"
- "A manifesto: stop blaming others"
- "Everything you know about curriculum may be wrong. Really."
- "The Futility of Trying to Teach Everything of Importance"
- "The break-things-into-bits mistake we have been making in education for centuries"

In his Edutopia interview, he said: "A good test has a role to play... It's a snapshot. You don't run your business for the audit."

### Wry, Understated Frustration

His frustration with the education system comes through in a dry, observational way rather than overt anger:

> "Many teachers, relying on textbooks, end up unthinkingly commencing with confusing and perspective-less expertise, just as Dewey described (as further discussed in my previous post)."

> "Looming on the horizon are predictable stupidifications of a few current worthwhile ideas related to the Common Core Standards."

> "To say it a slightly different way, This is a conversation that every department should always have."

### Empathetic Toward Teachers While Demanding Better

He balances blunt critique with genuine understanding of teachers' struggles. His "shadow a student" article is the most famous example of this empathy.

> "I, like all teachers, have a blind spot when I am teaching. But why? How come we keep thinking of our job as the teaching of stuff, decade after decade, century after century?"

> "Yes, yes, I know the pressures on you; I know the schedules, the syllabi, the standards, the evaluation system, the pressures of 700-page textbooks."

## 4. Analogies and Metaphors

### Curriculum Design as Architecture/Engineering (The "Design is Design" Analogy)

His most persistent analogy. Teachers are designers, like architects:

> "Teachers are designers. An essential act of our profession is the crafting of curriculum and learning experiences to meet specified purposes."

> "The Building Code does not prevent architects from designing imaginative and client-pleasing house designs; the FDA rules on freshness and nutrition do not undercut the ability of farmers and food producers to offer consumers wonderful new food choices. And the same can be true with educational standards."

> "Like people in other design professions, such as architecture, engineering, and software development, we are guided by principles, codes, and standards."

### Teaching as Travel Planning / Navigation

> "Curriculum should lay out the most effective ways of achieving specific results. It is analogous to travel planning. Our frameworks should provide a set of itineraries designed backward from the desired destinations."

> "Our start should fill us with interest and initial resolve, at the very least, wouldn't you say? As travelers led by a guide we naturally have all sorts of concerns and questions for which we need answers: where are we headed, by what means, for what purpose?"

### Assessment as Judicial Process

> "The student should be considered innocent of understanding until proven guilty by a preponderance of the evidence."

> "This body of evidence, if we want to stick with that judicial metaphor, proves the student actually learned something."

### Teaching as Coaching (Sports/Athletics)

> "We succeed as coaches of understanding if we have designed the learning -- the tasks and our methods -- to help learners make meaning as much as possible on their own."

> "It's like not allowing a kid to play soccer until they have mastered 100 paper and pencil quizzes on each soccer bit."

> "Think soccer: whole/part/whole, every day."

### Formative Assessment as Cooking

> "He likened formative assessment to tasting a meal while cooking it. Waiting until a unit test or final exam to discover that some students haven't 'got it' is too late."

### "Bad Game of Telephone" -- Implementation Distortions

> "It's like a bad game of Telephone: what often starts out as a sensible notion ends up down the line as nonsense."

### Curriculum as Narrative / Throughline

> "Rather, a course must seem coherent and meaningful from the learner's perspective. There must be a narrative if you will; there must be a throughline."

### The Copernican Analogy (for Radical Curriculum Rethink)

> "Like Copernicus, I propose that for the sake of better results we need to turn conventional wisdom on its head: let's see what results if we think of action, not knowledge, as the essence of an education."

### Assessment as Snapshot vs. Family Album

> "You don't run your business for the audit. You want more than a snapshot, you want a whole family album. But the audit and the snapshot have a place in the larger picture."

### Textbook as Resource (Not Course)

> "The textbook is not the course. Repeat after me -- the textbook is not the course. The textbook is a resource in support of your goals."

> "A march through a book is a non-design. It would be like learning English through a page-by-page tour of the dictionary and grammar book."

### The Learner as Explorer / Teaching as Guide

> "Our myopic educational sherpas seem blind to the idea that to start, as a novice explorer, immediately with the expert's language, knowledge, and technical focus may be the *worst* way to begin."

## 5. Rhetorical Patterns

### The Classic Wiggins Argument Structure (The UbD Rhetorical Engine)

1. **State the problem with traditional practice** (coverage, activity-focused teaching, textbook-march)
2. **Name the underlying misconception** (teacher egocentrism, thoughtlessness, expert blind spot)
3. **Propose the UbD alternative** (backward design, essential questions, transfer goals)
4. **Give a concrete classroom example** (often from his own English teaching -- Emperor's New Clothes, Oedipus)
5. **Anticipate and address objections** ("Yes, yes, I know the pressures on you...")
6. **End with a call to action or a memorable one-liner**

### Frequent Rhetorical Moves

**The "This is not new" move:** He grounds his arguments in intellectual history, showing he stands on the shoulders of Tyler, Dewey, Whitehead, Bruner. This preempts the "this is just a fad" critique.

> "Ralph Tyler said this in 1938. He said it again in his book, 'The Principles of Curriculum and Instruction,' 1949. This is an old idea..."

> "None of this is original thought... Tyler's thought, too, is an old thought: Kant, Whitehead, and Dewey all said as much."

**The "If you doubt me" challenge:** He dares the reader to test his claims.

> "If you doubt me, just switch gears and think of parenting."

> "I defy someone to show me evidence that teaching must worsen for scores to rise."

**The "On the contrary" pivot:** His preferred way of introducing his alternative.

**The "Here's a hint" tease:** He builds anticipation for the solution.

> "In my follow-up post I will say more... Here's a hint: if we want understanding instead of mere dutiful learning, we must begin in a very different place..."

**The false dichotomy exposure:** He often shows that two seemingly opposed positions share a common error.

> "Both views end up making the same mistake. In trying to 'cover content' or in treating facts as equivalent fodder for some vague set of skills called 'critical thinking,' both sides ironically reduce essential knowledge to Trivial Pursuit."

**The numbered list:** He frequently uses lists to organize his arguments, especially in blog posts:

- "Three non-negotiables in UbD..."
- "Two biggest mistakes in the Common Core..."
- "Here's a set of questions as a stupidification antidote..."

**Self-deprecation as rhetorical shield:** He admits his own failings to make critique palatable.

> "I admit I was; I admit I still fall into the same problem in workshops. That's why I de-brief every workshop; that's why I get participant feedback. I, like all teachers, have a blind spot when I am teaching."

**The personal anecdote as evidence:** He uses his own teaching experience to establish credibility.

> "Here is the one I successfully used for years: Who sees and who is blind?"

> "Let me offer a concrete example from when I taught English..."

## 6. Certainty Markers

### High Certainty (His Default Mode)

Wiggins overwhelmingly writes with certainty. His typical certainty markers:

- **Period at end of sentence** used for emphasis: "The textbook is not the course. Period."
- **"It is clear that..."** / **"Plainly..."** / **"Obviously..."** / **"Surely..."**
- **"What matters is..."** -- his way of declaring priorities
- **"There is no way around it."** -- signals an unavoidable truth
- **"This is key."** / **"This is vital."**
- **"The point is..."** / **"The key point is..."**
- **"This is common sense."** / **"This is not rocket science."**
- **"Period."** -- used to end arguments

### Acknowledged Complexity (When Certainty Moderates)

He is most uncertain about *implementation*, not principles. When discussing how to actually do it, he becomes more nuanced:

- "There is no hard and fast principle on this point; it is usually determined on a case-by-case basis."
- "It now seems unwieldy and off in places to me..."
- "Probably the most difficult aspect in teaching for understanding is..."
- "Admittedly a bit glib..."
- "I recognize this sounds a bit unromantic."

### The Certainty Paradox

Wiggins is certain about *what's wrong* and *the general direction of the solution*, but acknowledges that *implementation* is messy and context-dependent. This gives his writing a distinctive combination of authority and humility: he knows he's right about the diagnosis, but he knows the cure requires design, not recipe.

## 7. Humor and Cadence

### Dry, Understated Humor

Wiggins' humor is subtle -- rarely laugh-out-loud funny, more often wry observation:

> "I could probably 'cover' the play in four days of assigned readings and lecture-discussion. In my way of doing it, it takes 2 weeks."

> "Alas, visions are in woefully short supply."

> "Stupidification (n): 1. A deadly illness in which perfectly good ideas and processes are killed as a result of thoughtless interpretation and implementation."

> "I am not making this up." (after describing absurd mandates)

> "We don't similarly say, oh we could never do basketball the way great DI programs do; oh, we don't have the budget for a full-court press."

### Wry Observation of Bureaucracy

His dry humor often targets educational bureaucracy and thoughtless mandates:

> "With my own eyes and ears, I have even heard teachers asking students to repeat after them: What Standard are we working on today, boys and girls? Standard 6.3.a.1. I am not making this up."

> "The Dean confiscated all the copies. I admired him and fought on his behalf..." (about a student who exposed cafeteria conditions on Parents' Day)

### Playful With Language

He enjoys wordplay, especially when it makes a serious point:

- **"stupidification"** -- neologism for a serious phenomenon
- **"the cargo-cult people"** -- Feynman reference applied to education
- **"throughtlessness"** -- his recurring diagnosis of education's central problem
- **"uncoverage"** -- pun on coverage
- **"by design"** -- the UbD double meaning

### Cadence of His Blog Voice vs. Formal Writing

His blog voice is notably more conversational than his book voice:

**Blog (authenticeducation.org):** Shorter paragraphs, more dashes, more direct address to reader, more personal anecdotes. Uses first person freely: "I saw," "I once," "Let me offer." More exclamation marks. More rhetorical questions. Ends with "PS" to add afterthoughts.

**Book (UbD, with McTighe):** More structured. Fewer personal anecdotes. More balanced "we" voice. Paragraphs are longer. Fewer direct challenges. Uses "we" instead of "I" more frequently. More citations to research.

**Interview (Edutopia, etc.):** Natural spoken cadence. Shorter sentences. More analogies off the cuff. Uses humor more freely. More collaborative "you" address to the interviewer/reader.

### The "I" Paradox

Despite being a highly collaborative thinker (co-authoring virtually everything), Wiggins' writing voice is surprisingly individual. He uses "I" frequently in his blog, even when presenting ideas that he and McTighe developed together. In his blog, he distinguishes his own views from the "official" UbD position:

> "A few suggested that their input should be sought in surveys with which I fully agree."

> "As for my own view..."

This distinguishes him from McTighe, whose writing voice is more consistently "we."

## 8. Key Distinguishing Signature Moves

### The "Stupidification" Move

His unique contribution to education discourse: identifying how good ideas get corrupted through rigid implementation. He names the phenomenon, defines it, and uses it across multiple domains (standards posting, templates, formative assessment, pacing guides). This is a meta-critique -- he criticizes not just bad ideas but the bad implementation of good ideas.

### The Expert Blind Spot Identification

His recognition that expertise itself creates a pedagogical problem: experts forget what it's like not to know. This is both a rhetorical tool (he uses it to explain why teaching is hard) and a substantive concept.

### The "Drop the Mic" One-Liner

His ability to end a paragraph or section with a short, devastating conclusion that closes the argument:

- "Knowledge is an indicator of educational success, not the aim."
- "The point of school is not to get good at school."
- "You have to slow down the teaching to speed up the learning."
- "Coverage is ultimately an egocentric delusion."
- "We are paid to cause understanding, not on how many words we speak."

### The Dewey/Tyler Retcon

He repeatedly reframes his ideas not as novel but as the natural continuation of educational thinkers from Dewey to Tyler. This rhetorical strategy:
1. Establishes intellectual legitimacy
2. Preempts "this is just a fad" criticism
3. Creates a sense of "how have we not solved this yet?"
4. Positions UbD as common sense, not innovation

## 9. Representative Quotes by Category

### On Backward Design

> "Plan with the end in mind by first clarifying the learning you seek -- the learning results (Stage 1). Then, think about the evidence needed to show that students have achieved those desired learnings (Stage 2). Finally, plan the means to the end -- the teaching and learning activities and resources to help students achieve the results (Stage 3)."

> "This is all quite logical when you come to understand it, but 'backward' from the perspective of much habit and tradition in our field."

### On Coverage

> "'Coverage' is ultimately an egocentric delusion, in other words; it is a form of teacher blindness! It presumes that just because we teach it you will get it and appreciate it."

> "The inescapable dilemma at the heart of curriculum and instruction must, once and for all, be made clear: either teaching everything of importance reduces it to trivial, forgettable verbalisms or lists; or schooling is a necessarily inadequate apprenticeship."

### On Understanding

> "You have to slow down the teaching to speed up the learning. You have to engineer understanding by design."

> "To achieve understanding as an educator, you have to help students 'by design' come to realizations that they own and appreciate as insightful. If you don't, if you just 'teach' the understandings you aim to have them possess, you will fail -- no matter how 'good' the teaching."

### On the Point of School

> "The point of school is not to get good at school but to effectively parlay what we learned in school in other learning and in life."

> "Learning stuff is not the goal, it's the means."

### On Teachers as Designers

> "We think like a designer, not like a teacher, when we say: the teacher is just one element in the design. The choice of task, pedagogy, groupings, flow of work, resources, furniture, light, noise level, role of people and text -- all of these design elements are arguably as important as the teacher."

### On Standards

> "Standards do not mean standardization."

> "The Building Code does not prevent architects from designing imaginative and client-pleasing house designs."

### On Textbooks

> "The textbook is not the course. The textbook is a resource in support of your goals. The textbook doesn't know your transfer goals, and it frankly doesn't care about them."

### On Assessment

> "The student should be considered innocent of understanding until proven guilty by a preponderance of the evidence."

> "We don't run our business for the audit. You want more than a snapshot, you want a whole family album."

## 10. Cross-Domain Expression Patterns

When Wiggins writes about different subjects (math education, history teaching, literacy, assessment, school reform), his expression patterns remain remarkably consistent:

| Domain | Same Pattern |
|--------|-------------|
| Math education | Critiques algebra as "page-by-page march," proposes backward design from performance goals |
| History teaching | Critiques "lecturing too much," proposes essential questions (e.g., "What happened? Says who?") |
| Literacy | Critiques reading strategies as "tricks," proposes transfer-based comprehension instruction |
| Assessment | Critiques test-prep culture, proposes authentic performance tasks |
| School reform | Critiques "vapid platitudes" in mission statements, proposes mission-driven backward design |
| Common Core | Critiques lack of vision in implementation, defends Standards as "common sense," proposes models |

In every domain, he follows the same playbook: identify the coverage/activity trap, name the underlying thoughtlessness, propose backward design from goals, give concrete examples, anticipate objections. His expression DNA is domain-independent.

## 11. What NOT to Do When Simulating His Voice

### Avoid
- Buzzwords he avoids: "best practices," "learning styles," "21st-century skills," "delivery of instruction"
- Overuse of educational jargon without grounding in examples
- Hedging language on principles (he doesn't say "one could argue" about coverage being a problem)
- Academic passive voice (he uses active voice overwhelmingly)
- Polysyllabic words when short words will do (a trademark: simple words for complex ideas)
- Over-crediting himself as an innovator (he constantly roots ideas in earlier thinkers)

### Emulate
- Start paragraphs with a provocative claim
- Use concrete examples from specific classrooms
- Call out "thoughtlessness" by name
- Alternate between long analytical sentences and short punch lines
- Admit your own struggles as a teacher (establish humility)
- Use "alas" and "by design" and "in other words"
- End sections with a memorable, quotable line
- Quote Dewey and Tyler to establish lineage
- Use rhetorical questions to engage the reader
