## 2. UbD Adoption in Schools and Districts

- Over 250,000 educators own the *Understanding by Design* book; over 30,000 handbooks are in use; more than 150 university education classes use it as a text (Wiggins, cited in Ronda, 2010).
- ASCD reports "thousands of educators worldwide are already using Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe's Understanding by Design (UbD) as a framework for designing curriculum units, performance assessments, and instruction."
- The framework is used across K-12, higher education, and professional schools.

### School District Examples
- **Minnetonka Public Schools (MN):** Uses UbD as their official instructional framework. "In Minnesota, Districts must make local decisions about how state standards will be taught, and we use the backward-mapping Understanding by Design (UbD) process." (minnetonka.k12.mn.us)

- **Herscher CUSD #2 (IL):** Official UbD adoption.

- **Bucks County, PA:** Multiple districts adopted UbD for K-12 curriculum development.

### Medical and Higher Education Adoption

## 3. Common Critiques of UbD

### Critique A: "Backwards Design Is Too Prescriptive/Rigid"

**TeachThought (Terry Heick, 2019):**
- Argues backward planning "doesn't 'center' understanding" on its own - "The priorities here are sequence and alignment, not wisdom and thought."
- Raises concern that backward planning "crowd[s] out the teacher-supported humanization and data-supported personalization of learning."
- Questions whether backward design is "the best way to personalize learning for students."
- Notes that "by dictating so precisely what a student will come to know, we are narrowing the accepted boundaries of their learning."

**Academic critique (Cho & Trent, ERIC EJ795704, 2005):**
- From a poststructural perspective, backward design relies on "purposeful task analysis" which is "typical in positivist discourse."
- Questions "the structural or positivist assumption that students' deeper understanding can be achieved by design, or purposeful task analysis."
- Identifies both Tyler's rationale and UbD as beginning with "predetermined outcomes" and following a linear model.

**Beyond Backward Design (TIA Journal, 2022):**
- Describes "backward design's lockstep reasoning, which assumes a strict means-ends pedagogical logic."
- Notes the critique that "learning outcomes have become so indebted to the bureaucratic requirements of specificity and measurability that they have become largely irrelevant to actual classroom practice."
- Argues what was introduced in the 1990s "as a way to interrupt dogmatism in curriculum may now have become a kind of dogma."

**ASCD's own response:**
- The UbD white paper explicitly states UbD offers "a planning process and structure" and "helps this process without offering a rigid process or prescriptive recipe."
- Wiggins himself acknowledged implementation problems in his 2014 post "12 Ways to Kill Understanding by Design (UbD) from the Start," warning against mandating UbD for all planning immediately, standardizing all implementation, and fixating on terminology.

### Critique B: "Essential Questions Are Too Abstract"

**Mathematics for Teaching (Erlina Ronda, 2010):**
- Calls essential questions "elusive questions that teachers have difficulty formulating since probably the time the education community was talking about 'art of questioning.'"
- Argues that identifying enduring understandings and formulating good questions requires strong content knowledge (CK) and pedagogical content knowledge (PCK) that many teachers lack.
- Suggests training money would be better spent on deepening content knowledge rather than UbD training.

**4QM Teaching (history education, 2019):**
- Reports that essential questions in history curriculum documents "are pretty much the exact opposite of 'essential.'"
- "Most teachers and students ignore them — they make no difference at all to classroom teaching and learning."
- Dirty secret: "We kinda just added those on, and no one really asks kids to answer them."

**Elliott Seif (Curriculum development writer, 2023):**
- Notes that "when teachers use UbD to redesign units of study, they fail to select, narrow down and prioritize understandings and essential questions to those that are most important, relevant, and compelling."
- Finds that understandings and essential questions are sometimes not aligned - "not connected and related to each other."

**Wiggins' own response:**
- In "How To Use Essential Questions In Your Lesson Design" (2015), Wiggins acknowledged: "Far too often over the years, I have seen plenty of good stuff posted like this — but no deep embedding of the Essential Question (EQ) into the unit design."
- "The only person that keeps referring to the EQ is the teacher" - EQs are rarely central to assessment.
- Co-authored *Essential Questions: Doorways to Understanding* to address these implementation problems.

### Critique C: "UbD Works Better for Some Subjects Than Others"

**Mathematics for Teaching (Ronda, 2010):**
- Argues the six facets of understanding (explain, interpret, apply) "does not capture what it means to understand mathematics."
- "UbD is a one size fits all for all subject areas. That's what make it highly suspect."
- Questions whether advance organizers work for all teaching methods: "If the approach is Teaching through Problem Solving or Discovery method, this is a no-no as it might limit the students thinking."

**ASCD UbD White Paper (2014):**
- Addresses the question: "Is the UbD framework appropriate for mathematics?"
- Notes "some educators have questioned the use of the UbD framework in mathematics (and other skill-focused areas, such as world languages or early literacy)."
- The concern: "the UbD framework seems to stress understanding to the exclusion of basic knowledge and skills."
- ASCD response: "The suggestion that the UbD framework does not recognize the need for learners to develop basic knowledge and skills could not be further from the truth!" The template explicitly calls for "what students should know (e.g., multiplication tables) and be able to do (e.g., division)."

**Philippine implementation (Inquirer, 2011):**
- Math teachers in the Philippines struggled with mapping six facets of understanding to mathematical topics.
- Example: Students were asked to show empathy by "describing the difficulties one can experience without knowing the rules for special products."
- A nun commented: "Many people live without knowing special products. I doubt they need our empathy."

**Empirical support for UbD in math:**
- A quasi-experimental study in a Swiss vocational school showed UbD-based math teaching outperformed traditional approaches, with effects lasting at least one school year (New Zealand Journal of Teachers' Work).
- Multiple studies confirm UbD positively impacts mathematics conceptual understanding and problem-solving skills.

### Critique D: "Too Much Planning Time Required"

**Illinois Teacher Guide (2021):**
- "Teachers and administrators who use the UbD backward design process cite both its strengths and weaknesses. Its primary strength is that it takes an obvious, clear, and intentional approach to curricular design. Its primary weakness is the time required for teachers and administrators to implement the approach."
- Recommends: "Implement UbD in small, manageable segments over a longer period of time."

**Uluçınar Meta-Synthesis (2021):**
- Reviewed 48 UbD studies from 6 databases, included 12 in analysis.
- Found teachers struggled with UbD due to "model inexperience, the school's facilities/conditions, teacher stagnation and emotional state, and inadequate pedagogy knowledge."
- Challenges included "intensity of their workload and limited time."

**Eduplanet21 (2020):**
- "UbD is not an easy planning process."
- Recommends "planning two or three units a year as a start. Then, expand to additional units in future years."

## 4. Teacher Feedback: Practical Experiences

### Positive Teacher Feedback

**Resilient Educator (2015):**
- A teacher describes UbD as transformative: "It changed the foundations of my own instruction."
- UbD reduced "sage on the stage" time, improved transfer and skill acquisition.
- "Rather than abandoning lessons, I'm able to revisit and tweak them to realign the work toward my educational goals."

**DLSZ High School Study (Philippines, 2015):**
- Teachers perceived the UbD framework as "useful in writing learning modules."
- Found it helped in "determining the learning goals, constructing assessments and preparation of different learning activities."
- Students "demonstrate understanding of the desired goals through the performance tasks or products."

**Indonesian English Teachers Study (2024):**
- "Majority of teachers have a good to very good understanding of the UbD and Backward Design concepts."
- Most have implemented UbD and "demonstrate a high commitment to self-development in applying UbD."
- Suggests need for more intensive support and continuous training.

**Teacher Blog (Dana Huff, huffenglish.com):**
- Found UbD "freeing" - it helped her stay "out of the front of the classroom."
- Reports using the WHERE elements of UbD to design better projects.
- Believes UbD creates "semester-long inquiry" experiences.

### Challenges Reported by Teachers

**Time constraints:**
- Consistently cited across all studies as the primary barrier.
- Illinois guide: "Despite its clear benefits and common-sense approach, the question of how to implement UbD with limited time is still present."

**Philippines implementation problems:**
- Teachers in 10+ schools "could not finish the topics for a quarter" under UbD.
- "Many teachers stopped using the guides, because they would end up using the old approach anyway."
- Majority of divisions in the National Capital Region abandoned UbD because "students will take the National Achievement Test in March and, with UBD, teachers will not be able to finish the competencies."
- Only higher-section students used UbD; others received traditional instruction.

**Content knowledge requirements:**
- Ronda argues UbD demands strong content knowledge that many teachers lack.
- "Shouldn't the money and time for training teachers how to design a lesson using UbD be spent instead on deepening their understanding of CK and PCK?"

**Pre-service teacher challenges:**
- A Texas study (2020) showed pre-service teachers improved lesson design knowledge through UbD training.
- However, challenges remain in "aligning UbD components with students' learning behaviors and real-world classroom contexts."
- Meta-synthesis found teachers "generally did not create goals/standards by collecting evaluation evidence" due to inexperience.

## 5. Connections Between UbD and Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

### Complementary Frameworks
- UbD and UDL are widely described as complementary. "Backward design sharpens goals and evidence. UDL anticipates learner differences and plans flexible paths." (Education Blog Desk, 2026)
- Both are used together in K-10 classrooms, though they have different origins and emphases.

### Key Differences
- **UbD** focuses on curriculum design - WHAT we teach, WHY we teach it, and HOW we assess. Developed by Wiggins and McTighe (late 1990s).
- **UDL** focuses on accessibility and learner variability - HOW to make instruction accessible to ALL learners. Developed by CAST (Center for Applied Special Technology).
- UbD does not explicitly focus on designing instruction accessible to learners with disabilities; UDL is built on that principle.

### How They Work Together
- Stage 1 (UbD) + UDL: "Develop clear learning goals, then use UDL to consider potential barriers and design flexible paths."
- Stage 2 (UbD) + UDL: "Plan assessments aligned with goals, using multiple means of expression."
- Stage 3 (UbD) + UDL: "Plan instruction using multiple means of representation, action, and engagement."

### Published Integration Guides
- Tomlinson & McTighe (2006): *Integrating Differentiated Instruction and Understanding by Design: Connecting Content and Kids* (ASCD). "UbD and DI: An Essential Partnership."
- The Equity Edge (2023): "From Understanding by Design (UbD) to Universal Design of Learning (UDL): A comparative analysis of design frameworks for teaching diverse K-10 learners."
- Know, Do, Understand (KDU) framework: Grounded in UbD, often combined with UDL for inclusive standards-based planning.

### Practical Use in Schools
- Pennsbury School District's curriculum renewal manual includes both UbD and UDL as frameworks.
- UDL is increasingly seen as "an umbrella that aligns many initiatives" including UbD, MTSS, DI, and PBIS.
- The UDL Schoolwide Implementation Criteria (UDL-SIC) positions UDL as an overarching framework, with UbD as a complementary design process within it.

## 8. Comparisons with Other Curriculum Design Frameworks

### UbD vs. Tyler Rationale (Ralph Tyler, 1949)

**Similarities:**
- Both begin with predetermined outcomes/objectives.
- Both follow a linear, sequential process.
- Both use backward logic: start with ends, then plan means.
- The seminal UbD book (1998) is explicitly grounded in Tyler's tradition.

**Differences:**
- Tyler's model has four steps: objectives, experiences, organization, evaluation.
- UbD has three stages: desired results, evidence, learning plan.
- UbD uniquely adds the "six facets of understanding" and "essential questions" as explicit design elements.
- Tyler focused on behavioral objectives; UbD focuses on understanding and transfer.

**Critique shared by both:**
- Both critiqued as "positivist" and overly linear (poststructural critique).
- Both assume understanding can be achieved through systematic design.
- Both criticized for being "prescriptive" and potentially narrowing learning.

### UbD vs. Taba's Grassroots Model (Hilda Taba, 1962)

**Differences:**
- Tyler and UbD are deductive (top-down); Taba's model is inductive (grassroots, bottom-up).
- Taba emphasizes teacher-led curriculum development; UbD is often implemented as a top-down district framework.
- Taba's model has 7 steps and is more flexible; UbD's 3-stage process is more structured.
- Taba prioritizes teacher autonomy; UbD prioritizes alignment with standards.

**Critique context:**
- Taba's grassroots approach is described as "time-consuming" and "difficult to apply uniformly across all subjects" (Teachers Institute, 2025).
- UbD is described as focusing on "deep understanding and transfer of learning" but "requires significant planning and may be challenging to implement."

### UbD vs. Concept-Based Curriculum (H. Lynn Erickson)

**Connections:**
- UbD's "enduring understandings" are similar to Erickson's "generalizations."
- Both emphasize conceptual thinking over factual recall.
- Both use "big ideas" as organizing principles.
- One study notes: "The UbD approach also places much emphasis on concept-based 'enduring understandings,' which are similar to what Erickson (2007) calls 'generalizations.'"
- Both frameworks used together in concept-based curriculum design.

### UbD vs. Saylor-Alexander-Lewis Model

- Saylor-Alexander-Lewis serves administrative needs for "schools and districts managing curriculum at scale."
- UbD is more focused on individual teacher/team unit design.
- Both emphasize alignment but from different institutional levels.

### Summary Comparison Table (from Teachers Institute, 2025)

| Model | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| Tyler's Rationale | Clear structure, logical sequence | May be too rigid, fails to account for diverse learners |
| Taba's Grassroots | Empowers teachers, participatory | Time-consuming, depends on teacher skill |
| Understanding by Design (UbD) | Focuses on deep understanding & transfer | Requires significant planning, challenging to implement |
| Miller & Seller's Orientations | Examines underlying values | Less a process, more a lens |

### UbD's Place in the Landscape
- UbD is the most widely adopted contemporary curriculum design framework in US K-12 education (2020s).
- It is described as "standards-based" and "outcomes-driven," fitting within the Tyler tradition.
- It is increasingly adopted in higher education, medical education, and international contexts.
- Compared to Tyler, UbD adds a richer conception of understanding (six facets) and a more practical template for unit design.
- Compared to Erickson, UbD is more focused on assessment design (Stage 2) and backward planning logic.

## Summary of Key Findings

1. **UbD remains highly influential post-2015.** McTighe continues Wiggins' work, partnerships with publishers and software companies keep the framework alive, and the books remain in active publication.

2. **Adoption is widespread but uneven.** UbD is used in K-12 districts across the US, internationally (New Zealand, Philippines, Indonesia, Turkey), and increasingly in medical and professional education. However, implementation quality varies significantly.

3. **Common critiques have merit but are partially addressed.** Wiggins and McTighe acknowledged the time demands and implementation pitfalls. The critique about essential questions being abstract reflects poor implementation rather than framework flaws. The subject-specific critique has been contested with empirical evidence.

4. **Teacher feedback is mixed.** Teachers who implement UbD well report transformative effects. Many struggle with time constraints, content knowledge demands, and tension between understanding goals and coverage requirements (especially in high-stakes testing environments).

5. **UbD and UDL are increasingly used together.** The complementarity is well-recognized: UbD provides the "what" and "why" of curriculum design; UDL provides the "how" of inclusive access.

6. **Recent scholarship confirms UbD's effectiveness** in improving cognitive development, student engagement, and learning permanence, especially in science and mathematics.
