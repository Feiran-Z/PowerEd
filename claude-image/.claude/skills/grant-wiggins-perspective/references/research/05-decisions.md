# Grant Wiggins: Key Career Decisions and What They Reveal

> Research compiled April 30, 2026. Sources include Authentic Education archives, ASCD publications, Education Week, Edutopia, and other publicly available interviews and articles.

---

## Decision 1: Choosing St. John's College (Great Books Program) over Conventional Education Paths

### Context
Wiggins graduated high school in the late 1960s. He chose St. John's College in Annapolis (1968-1972), a school with a unique "Great Books" curriculum where there are no electives and students read primary texts (Euclid, Newton, Plato, etc.) in seminars. This is arguably the most distinctive undergraduate education in America.

### What Was Decided
He pursued a BA from St. John's College rather than a more conventional or professionally-oriented undergraduate education.

### Alternatives
He could have attended a standard university with education-focused or pre-professional tracks.

### Outcome
Wiggins later wrote: "I was educated in the classic tradition at St. John's College. I learned physics and calculus through Newton's Principia and geometry through Euclid and Lobachevski—in a college program with no electives—all based on the Great Books. I had arguably the best undergraduate education in the United States, if the aim is intellectual power."

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Depth over breadth**: This experience shaped his lifelong conviction that deep engagement with foundational ideas matters more than superficial coverage of content.
- **Skepticism of standardisation**: Having experienced an unconventional education, he was never a defender of the status quo.
- **Intellectual seriousness**: The Great Books approach taught him that learning is about wrestling with primary sources and big questions—a theme that runs through all his work (essential questions, big ideas, uncoverage).
- **Critical note**: Despite his praise for St. John's, he actively resisted the idea that its model should be mandated for all. He wrote: "Would I mandate that all colleges look like St. John's? Absolutely not." This reveals a pragmatism that balanced his idealism—he believed in offering rich options, not imposing one model.

## Decision 2: Pursuing Ed.D. at Harvard While Teaching

### Context
Wiggins taught high school English and philosophy for 14 years (approximately 1972-1986). During that time, he simultaneously pursued his Ed.M. and Ed.D. at the Harvard Graduate School of Education (1981-1987). He was both a practitioner and a scholar.

### What Was Decided
He chose to earn a doctorate from Harvard's education school while remaining grounded in classroom practice, rather than pursuing a purely academic PhD or leaving teaching for full-time graduate study.

### Alternatives
- Full-time academic PhD in education or a discipline
- Stay in classroom teaching without advanced degree
- Leave teaching for administration immediately

### Outcome
His Ed.D. gave him academic credibility while his 14 years of teaching gave him practitioner credibility. This dual identity defined his entire career: he was always an advocate for teachers but also willing to critique them.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Theory-practice integration**: He refused the false choice between being a scholar and being a practitioner.
- **Grassroots credibility mattered**: He wanted to be able to say "I've been in the classroom" and mean it. His recommendations always carried the weight of experience.
- **Ambition for systemic change**: The Ed.D. suggests he saw himself not just as a classroom teacher but as someone who could reshape how schooling works at a structural level.

## Decision 3: Leaving Classroom Teaching for Research and Consulting

### Context
After 14 years as a secondary English and philosophy teacher and coach, Wiggins left the classroom to work with Ted Sizer's Coalition of Essential Schools (CES) at Brown University in the late 1980s. He became the Director of Research on Curriculum and Teaching for CES.

### What Was Decided
He transitioned from classroom teacher to researcher, writer, and consultant—a move from working within one classroom to trying to influence many.

### Alternatives
- Continue as a classroom teacher (his love for teaching was real)
- Move into school administration
- Take a university faculty position

### Outcome
This decision launched his national career. At CES, he developed the concepts of "essential questions," "authentic assessment," and "uncoverage." He published his landmark article "A True Test: Toward More Authentic and Equitable Assessment" (Phi Delta Kappan, 1989) and "Teaching to the (Authentic) Test" (Educational Leadership, 1989). These pieces became foundational texts of the assessment reform movement.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Scalable impact**: He believed changing one classroom at a time was insufficient; he wanted to change the system's underlying logic.
- **Intellectual restlessness**: He was not content to perfect his own practice; he needed to think about why practice was broken at a structural level.
- **Risk-taking**: Leaving a stable teaching career for the uncertain world of educational consulting and research required courage.
- **Part of a movement**: Joining CES aligned him with Ted Sizer's reform vision—personalised schools, depth over coverage, students as workers, teachers as coaches. These ideas permeate Wiggins' later work.

## Decision 4: Co-founding the Center on Learning, Assessment, and School Structure (CLASS)

### Context
In the late 1980s or early 1990s, Wiggins founded CLASS, a nonprofit educational research and consulting organization based in Pennington/Princeton, New Jersey. He served as president and director of programs.

### What Was Decided
Rather than working solely through CES or as an independent contractor, Wiggins created an institutional home for his reform ideas.

### Alternatives
- Stay at CES permanently
- Work as an independent consultant under his own name only
- Join a university faculty full-time

### Outcome
CLASS became the vehicle through which Wiggins:
- Consulted with schools, districts, and state education departments
- Led state-level assessment reform initiatives (Vermont portfolio project, New Jersey and North Carolina performance assessment consortia)
- Published books including *Assessing Student Performance* (1993) and *Educative Assessment* (1998)
- Developed the first iterations of what would become Understanding by Design

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Institution-building**: He understood that ideas without an institutional infrastructure to spread them remain academic exercises.
- **Applied focus**: CLASS was not a think tank that generated reports; it was a hands-on consulting organization that worked directly with educators.
- **Systems thinking**: The organization's very name—Center on Learning, Assessment, and **School Structure**—reveals his conviction that assessment reform is inseparable from school structure reform.

## Decision 5: Partnering with Jay McTighe on Understanding by Design

### Context
Wiggins met Jay McTighe, then director of the Maryland Assessment Consortium, through their shared work in assessment reform. They recognized complementary strengths.

### What Was Decided
They co-authored *Understanding by Design* (1998), published by ASCD, and continued to collaborate on an entire franchise of UbD materials (expanded editions, workbooks, guides, professional development resources, and the book *Schooling by Design* in 2007).

### How They Divided Labor
Based on their biographies and contributions:
- **Wiggins** brought the theoretical framework, the assessment expertise, the rhetorical fire, and the big-picture critique of schooling. He was the visionary and provocateur.
- **McTighe** brought the practical design tools, the template, the school-district implementation experience, and the structured professional development approach. He was the systematizer and practitioner.
- McTighe described their relationship as "intellectual partner and best friend," saying "Although I am an only child, I considered Grant my brother."

### Alternatives
- Wiggins could have developed UbD independently
- He could have partnered with a university-based researcher
- He could have kept UbD as a consulting methodology rather than publishing it

### Outcome
UbD became one of the most influential curriculum design frameworks in the world, used in thousands of schools across the US and internationally. The partnership amplified both men's impact.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Collaboration over ego**: Wiggins was secure enough to share authorship and credit. UbD is always "Wiggins and McTighe," never "Wiggins' framework."
- **Valuing practical tools**: The partnership worked because Wiggins respected what McTighe brought—the templates, the structures, the implementation guidance that made the theory usable.
- **Publishing as activism**: Choosing ASCD as publisher put UbD in the hands of practicing educators, not just academics.

## Decision 6: Making Assessment Reform His First Major Focus

### Context
Wiggins' first major wave of national publications (1988-1993) focused almost entirely on assessment. His landmark 1989 Phi Delta Kappan article "A True Test: Toward More Authentic and Equitable Assessment" and his 1993 book *Assessing Student Performance* established him as the leading voice for "authentic assessment."

### What Was Decided
He chose assessment as the entry point for school reform rather than curriculum, instruction, or school structure.

### Why Assessment First
Three interconnected reasons:
1. **The "Trojan horse" strategy**: Wiggins believed assessment reform was the most powerful lever for changing everything else. He wrote: "assessment reform is the Trojan horse of real school reform." If you change what counts as evidence of learning, you force changes in curriculum, instruction, and grading.
2. **The shadow of the test**: Wiggins argued that "any test inevitably casts its shadow on all prior work." Tests don't just measure—they set standards. Reform the test, and you reform teaching.
3. **Personal experience as a teacher**: He had seen how standardized tests narrowed curriculum and rewarded superficial learning. He knew the problem from the inside.

### Alternatives
- Start with curriculum reform (he did get here with UbD, but assessment came first)
- Start with school structure (charter schools, school size, scheduling)
- Start with teacher preparation

### Outcome
He coined (or popularised) the term "authentic assessment," wrote the definitive theoretical case for it, and consulted on major state-level assessment reform initiatives including Vermont's portfolio project.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Strategic leverage**: He didn't just pick the most important problem—he picked the problem with the most leverage.
- **Pragmatic radicalism**: He was radical in his critique but strategic in his approach. Assessment was something schools already had to do; he wanted them to do it better.
- **Student-centeredness**: He believed assessment should primarily serve learners, not accountability systems.

## Decision 7: Using ASCD and Educational Leadership as His Primary Platform

### Context
Wiggins published prolifically in ASCD's journal *Educational Leadership* and through ASCD books. Key articles include "Teaching to the (Authentic) Test" (1989), "Standards, Not Standardization" (1991), "Creating Tests Worth Taking" (1992), and "Put Understanding First" (2008).

### What Was Decided
He chose ASCD (Association for Supervision and Curriculum Development)—a practitioner-oriented organization—as his primary publishing and professional development partner, rather than publishing primarily in academic journals.

### Alternatives
- Publish in peer-reviewed academic journals only
- Self-publish
- Publish with a commercial publisher not tied to professional development

### Outcome
ASCD's reach meant his ideas reached hundreds of thousands of practicing teachers and administrators. UbD became ASCD's best-selling product line.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Audience matters**: He wanted to reach teachers, not just other researchers.
- **Writing as a tool**: He wrote constantly—books, articles, blog posts, letters to the editor, Twitter threads. Writing was not a side activity but a core strategy for change.
- **Democratising ideas**: He made complex ideas accessible. UbD frames complex cognitive science in language a classroom teacher can use on Monday morning.

## Decision 8: Developing "Backward Design" as the Core of UbD

### Context
The concept of backward design—starting with desired results, then determining evidence, then planning instruction—was not new. Ralph Tyler had articulated a similar idea in 1949. The term "backward design" was used by sociologist James S. Coleman in 1990.

### What Was Decided
Wiggins and McTighe elevated backward design from a technical curriculum-planning concept to the defining framework of modern instructional design. They gave it a three-stage structure, a template, and a language that educators could use.

### What Problem Was He Trying to Solve
The "twin sins" of traditional curriculum design:
1. **Activity-oriented teaching**: Teachers design engaging activities without clarifying what students should learn from them.
2. **Coverage-oriented teaching**: Teachers march through content (textbooks, standards) without ensuring understanding.

Both sins treat teaching as an end rather than a means to learning.

### Alternatives
- Advocate for more engaging activities (more "fun" teaching)
- Advocate for better textbooks or standards
- Advocate for external accountability only

### Outcome
Backward design became the dominant curriculum planning paradigm in US education. UbD's three-stage template is now taught in most teacher preparation programs.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Clarity as a moral imperative**: He believed students deserve to know what they're aiming for. The opposite—secret standards, hidden expectations—was unethical.
- **Design thinking before it was trendy**: He treated curriculum as a design problem, not a content-delivery problem.
- **Teacher as designer**: His framework elevates teachers to the role of designers, not just implementers of others' plans.

## Decision 9: Navigating the Tension of Standardized Testing—Critic While Engaging the System

### Context
Wiggins was one of the most prominent critics of standardized testing in America. He argued that most standardized tests sacrificed validity for reliability, used proxy items instead of authentic performances, and caused "mind-numbing" test prep.

### What Was Decided
Rather than rejecting standardized testing entirely or advocating for its abolition, Wiggins:
1. **Acknowledged its necessity** for accountability purposes
2. **Called for better tests** that model authentic performance
3. **Argued for transparency**—releasing all test items, providing item-level analysis, letting teachers see what was tested
4. **Criticised his fellow educators** for producing even worse local assessments

### Key Quotes That Reveal His Nuanced Position
- "[C]urrent state accountability tests are insufficient to improve student and teacher performance."
- "Most local assessment, ironically, is of poorer quality - mostly simple factoids and plug and chug skills being sought."
- "I'm not sure I fully agree with the statement above"—responding to someone who assumed he would categorically oppose standardized tests. He was willing to acknowledge when standardized tests asked good questions.
- "The challenge is to offer kids a fabulous education; then, the test results take care of themselves."

### Alternatives
- Join the anti-testing movement wholeheartedly (as many of his progressive allies did)
- Ignore standardized tests entirely and focus only on authentic assessment
- Work within test companies to reform from inside

### Outcome
His nuanced position made him a bridge figure between progressive educators and accountability advocates. He was criticized by both sides—praised by the Fordham Institute for his willingness to engage with evidence, and defended by progressives for his commitment to authentic work.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Intellectual honesty over tribal loyalty**: He refused to be a partisan. He wrote an open letter to Diane Ravitch (when she was the leading anti-reform voice) arguing that "reform is strongly needed." He also criticized corporate education reform.
- **Evidence over ideology**: He followed the data even when it complicated his narrative.
- **High standards for everyone**: He held schools, teachers, test makers, and reformers to the same high standards. Nobody got a pass.

## Decision 10: Restructuring CLASS into Authentic Education (2002)

### Context
Wiggins founded Authentic Education (AE) in Hopewell, New Jersey, in 2002, transitioning from the nonprofit CLASS structure to a consulting firm. His wife, Denise Wilbur, became co-founder and later president of AE.

### What Was Decided
He created a private consulting organization that could:
- Provide sustained, long-term partnerships with schools and districts
- Develop workshops, resources, and training programs
- Train other consultants to spread UbD at scale
- Support international reform efforts (China, Philippines, Thailand)

### Alternatives
- Remain with CLASS as a purely nonprofit research organization
- Retire from consulting and focus on writing only
- Join a university

### Outcome
AE worked with thousands of schools, districts, and organizations worldwide. After Wiggins' death, Denise Wilbur led AE through 2022, then passed leadership to Mike Matthews in 2023. AE continues to this day.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Sustainability**: He built an organization that could outlast him.
- **Family**: Working with his wife Denise Wilbur suggests he valued partnership and continuity. She co-founded AE and led it after his death.
- **Global vision**: He took his ideas to China, the Philippines, Thailand—not just suburban American districts.

## Decision 11: His Approach to Intellectual Disagreement and Public Discourse

### Context
In his final years, Wiggins became increasingly active online through his blog "Granted, and...," Twitter, and public debates. He engaged vigorously with people who disagreed with him.

### What Was Decided
He chose to engage every critic, answer every email, and debate anyone willing to engage in "serious and spirited discourse." He called himself an "educational troublemaker of long standing."

### Key Examples
- **Open letter to Diane Ravitch**: Argued that "reform is strongly needed in many schools" and that the left's focus on privatization and poverty alone was insufficient.
- **Debate with Daniel Willingham**: His last blog posts (published the day before his death) challenged cognitive scientist Daniel Willingham on reading comprehension strategies.
- **Engagement with Robert Pondiscio (Fordham Institute)**: Pondiscio later wrote: "on several occasions, he'd write something about teaching or curriculum I vehemently disagreed with, or vice versa... If you disagreed with him... he had to know why."

### Outcomes
- He was widely respected even by ideological opponents.
- His willingness to engage made him a trusted figure across the education spectrum.
- His wife said his tribute was: "He came to embrace an opportunity to facilitate serious and spirited discourse about educational topics of import. Disagreement was welcome, but there had to be civil discourse and it needed to be well grounded by research and sound thinking."

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Truth-seeking over winning**: He did not debate to win; he debated to understand.
- **Accessibility**: "He answered every email, from the most august to the most humble, and he befriended many young educators seeking to make a difference."
- **Non-tribal identity**: He refused to join camps. He was called neither reformer nor anti-reformer; he just followed evidence and logic.
- **Character**: His intellectual honesty and personal warmth are consistently mentioned in tributes.

## Decision 12: Continuing to Work Until His Final Day

### Context
Wiggins died suddenly on May 26, 2015, at age 64, from a heart condition. The day before, he had published Part 2 of his response to Daniel Willingham on reading comprehension strategies. He was actively engaged in educational debate right up to the end.

### What Was Decided (Effectively)
He lived a life of total commitment to his work—not in the sense of workaholism, but in the sense that he never stopped thinking, writing, and engaging.

### How His Work Continued
- **Authentic Education (AE)** continued under his wife Denise Wilbur's leadership, transitioning to Mike Matthews as president in 2023.
- UbD remains ASCD's flagship curriculum design framework.
- The "Train the Trainer" program continues to help schools lead UbD work independently.
- AE has expanded into inclusive curriculum design, social studies standards development, and anti-racist curriculum work.
- His blog posts, articles, and books remain widely read and cited.

### What It Reveals About His Values
- **Lifelong learning**: He was still evolving his thinking until the end. His last debate was about reading research—not his core area.
- **Generosity**: He mentored countless educators. His impact is multi-generational.
- **Legacy through ideas, not institutional power**: He did not seek high office or political power. He sought to change how people think about teaching.

## Cross-Cutting Themes: What Wiggins' Decisions Reveal Overall

### 1. The Practitioner-Intellectual
He refused the false choice between theory and practice. He taught for 14 years, then spent 25 years consulting. His ideas always had to work in real classrooms.

### 2. Strategic Radicalism
He pushed radical ideas (authentic assessment, backward design, uncoverage) through mainstream channels (ASCD, Educational Leadership, school districts). He did not preach to the converted.

### 3. Intellectual Independence
He refused to join any camp—progressive or conservative, reformer or anti-reformer. He criticized teachers, test makers, policy makers, and reformers with equal energy.

### 4. Designing for Transfer (In His Own Life)
He transferred his learning from St. John's Great Books (big ideas, essential questions) to Harvard (research rigor) to the classroom (practical experience) to national reform (systems thinking). His life itself exemplifies the transfer he advocated.

### 5. Moral Seriousness About Learning
Beneath everything was a conviction that students deserve better—better assessments, better curriculum, better feedback, better teaching. "The point of school is not to get good at school." This moral clarity drove every professional decision.

### 6. Sustainability Through Systems
He built CLASS, then AE, then wrote books, then created templates and professional development. He didn't just advocate—he built infrastructure for change.
