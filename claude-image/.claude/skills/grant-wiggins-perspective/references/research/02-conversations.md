# Grant Wiggins: Interviews, Talks, and Presentations

## Overview of Wiggins' Speaking and Coaching Style

Grant Wiggins was known for a distinctive communication style that combined directness, pragmatism, and occasional bluntness with a deep commitment to improving student learning. His wife Denise Wilbur described him as embracing an "opportunity to facilitate serious and spirited discourse about educational topics of import," noting that "disagreement was welcome, but there had to be civil discourse and it needed to be well grounded by research and sound thinking." He called himself an "educational troublemaker of long-standing" and was described as someone who "believed in truth and in digging deep to find the truth, even when such digging made people uncomfortable. And it often did." (Education Week obituary, 2015)

Key traits of his speaking/teaching style:
- **Direct and often blunt**: "He didn't pull his punches" with friend and intellectual opponent alike (Education Week)
- **Heavy use of analogy**: Especially sports coaching analogies (soccer was his most frequent reference point)
- **Socratic in approach**: He constantly asked "If that's the goal, what follows?" to push audiences toward backward design thinking
- **Self-deprecating humor**: Openly admitted his own teaching failures and blind spots
- **Evidence-driven**: Always grounded arguments in research and data, not ideology
- **Generous with his time**: "He answered every email, from the most august to the most humble, and he befriended many young educators seeking to make a difference" (Denise Wilbur)

## 1. ASCD Conference Talks and Keynotes

### ASCD 2005 Conference on Teaching and Learning (San Francisco)
**Session title**: "Less Teaching, More Assessing"

One of Wiggins' most well-documented conference presentations. The core provocative message: "Teachers spend too much time teaching."

**Key quotes from this talk:**
- "We're paid to coach learners, not teach content."
- "This is common sense that we don't honor as teachers. We get fixated on the inputs, the content, and not enough on the desired output: resultant quality performance. The result is superficial."
- "I'm asking you to apply common sense to curriculum and assessment. It's going to overturn a lot of habits and traditions. Feedback and results are not the coin of the realm in most American schools; the textbook and the syllabus are."
- "I'm asking you to challenge the longstanding assumption that the syllabus, the framework, the pacing guide, the content is what matters. No, it's not. It's the results that matter. We need feedback against the desired results."

**Speaker observations**: Wiggins delivered this with a tone that acknowledged he was saying something provocative. The article covering the session notes it was "a concept guaranteed to provoke a reaction in a room full of teachers." He did not shy away from confrontation but framed his argument as "common sense" that educators simply weren't honoring. He used sports coaching analogies throughout, describing feedback systems as what coaches do naturally.

**On feedback specifically**: Wiggins distinguished between feedback, praise, and advice. He defined feedback crisply: "Feedback tells you what you just did. Feedback is information you can use. It's descriptive and useful information about what you did and didn't do in light of a goal." He noted that "'Good job!' is not feedback; it's praise."

**Source**: ASCD Educational Leadership article "Less Teaching, More Assessing" (2006), Vol. 48, No. 2

### ASCD Presentation: "Understanding by Design" (featured in Educational Leadership)
This was a presentation (likely a conference session or major talk) where Wiggins and McTighe introduced the backward design logic. Key moments captured:

- Wiggins introduced the memorable legal analogy: "The student should be considered innocent of understanding until proven guilty by a preponderance of the evidence."
- He told audiences to "think like an assessor, not like an activity designer"
- He emphasized: "We need to think of assessment reform as central to instructional reform, not just as making better assessments"
- On designing for understanding: "You must be able to predict students' misunderstandings, and design work to ferret them out and try to change them."

**Source**: ASCD Educational Leadership article "Understanding by Design" (archived online)

---

### NESA Conference Presentation: "On HS Math Reform" (2009)
A presentation delivered at NESA (Near East South Asia Council of Overseas Schools) with a detailed slide deck. Wiggins applied backward design to high school mathematics, using the essential question "What's Fair?" as an example. He demonstrated how to design units around authentic, engaging problems rather than textbook chapters.

**Key arguments:**
- The syllabus should be written before textbooks are chosen
- The syllabus should be based on standards, big ideas, and transfer goals - NOT textbook chapter sequences
- Teachers must decide which chapters to highlight, skim, or skip based on mission and standards

**Source**: NESA Conference handouts (WigginsMath.pdf, 2009)

## 2. Video Interviews and Recorded Workshops

### Avenues The World School Workshop: "Understanding by Design (1 of 2)" (2013)
**URL**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4isSHf3SBuQ
**Duration**: Approximately 20 minutes
**Format**: Live workshop with teachers

This is the most complete video record of Wiggins actually leading a teacher workshop. He is visible, interactive, and deeply engaged with participants.

**Key moments and direct quotes:**
- "UBD is not a philosophy of teaching, it's not an approach to teaching, it's a planning framework."
- "What you're trying to do is make it more likely by design that when you teach, you are more goal-focused, more effective."
- "What we see over and over again is that there is a misalignment between short-term plans and actions and long-term goals."
- When discussing assessment: "By assessing, I do not mean grading, I mean assessing just like you would do as a soccer coach. You're assessing, you're judging how we're doing against the goal, you're coaching, you're giving information about how we're doing against the goal."
- He repeatedly uses the refrain "If that's the goal, what follows?" as a structuring device for the entire workshop. He asks it about assessment, about instruction, about classroom activities - modeling backward design thinking in real time.

**Observations on style:**
- Wiggins is relaxed, conversational, and interactive
- He uses an informal, almost Socratic method of engaging the teacher audience
- He pauses frequently to let teachers discuss among themselves
- His tone is authoritative but not dictatorial - he positions himself as a fellow designer
- He is comfortable with silence and thinking time

### Edutopia Interview: "Grant Wiggins: Defining Assessment" (2002)
**URL**: https://edutopia.org/grant-wiggins-assessment
**Format**: Q&A interview

Seven questions covering testing vs. assessment, authentic assessment, backward design, project-based learning, technology, time constraints, and standardized testing.

**Key responses:**
- On the teach-test divide: "One of the challenges in teaching is designing, and to be a good designer you have to think about what you're trying to accomplish and craft a combination of the content and the instructional methods, but also the assessment."
- On backward design: "Instead of jumping to the activities -- 'Oh, I could have kids do this, oh, that'd be cool' -- you say, 'Well, wait a minute.' Before you decide exactly what you're going to do with them, if you achieve your objective, what does it look like? What's the evidence that they got it?"
- On time objections: "If you say you don't have time for assessment, you assume that the teaching is more important than the learning. Feedback is the key to reaching goals. Saying there's no time is to confuse causing learning with mentioning stuff."

**Observation**: Wiggins is patient and explanatory here, not confrontational. He anticipates teacher objections and addresses them directly.

### Edutopia Interview: "The Power of Backwards Design" (2013)
**Interviewer**: Ben Johnson
**URL**: https://www.edutopia.org/blog/interview-grant-wiggins-power-backwards-design-ben-johnson

**Key exchanges:**

On whether teachers have successfully adopted backward design:
"Following the concept of preparing the evaluation first (backward design), as proposed in Understanding by Design, is not a natural instinct and teachers are not doing as well as I might have hoped."

On what UbD requires vs. what people think it requires:
"UbD doesn't mean that you have to design the entire assessment before the lesson design, but at the very least you must think about the assessment before you design the instruction."

On the role of administrators:
Wiggins describes the "UbD Observation Form" he developed for supervisors. Key questions include: "What counts as evidence of learning success? What do you want me to look for in this observation?" He notes that when teachers cannot answer these questions, it is a warning sign visible in lesson delivery.

On the metaphor for teaching: Wiggins uses sports extensively - "just like in sports every non-conference game is formative" - to explain formative vs. summative assessment. Summative = championship game; formative = all the preparation leading up to it.

## 4. Q&A Sessions and Responses to Teacher Skepticism

### How do you plan? On templates and instructional planning (2012)
**Source**: Authentic Education blog

One of Wiggins' most revealing posts about pushback against UbD. He directly addressed the criticism that the UbD template is too rigid or constraining:

"We have hardly treated our own Template as a sacred untouchable icon. We have changed it 4 different times over the past 14 years, and we have provided examples in which various features of the Template were highlighted or left out. In short, we had zero intent of putting teachers in a mental and physical planning straitjacket."

On mandated templates by supervisors:
"Alas, some mandate-minded and unimaginative supervisors are still fitting all their teachers for one. So, I am not unhappy that Chicago schools recently decided to stop mandating a template for planning."

His candid self-critique:
"In retrospect, though, I think a mistake we made was to present one template instead of two or three. That would have perhaps better made the point that, while there needs to be a protocol and checklist to ensure that easily-overlooked elements are considered, there are none the less different yet acceptable ways of accomplishing this."

**Observation on handling rigidity pushback**: Wiggins acknowledged the problem, took partial responsibility, and offered concrete alternatives - templates, variations, flexibility. He distinguished between the intent of the design (ensuring key questions are asked) and the rigid implementation (mandating one template). This is a hallmark of his approach: he accepts valid criticism, adjusts, but does not abandon the core principles.

---

### UbD and Inquiry - A Response to 2 Questions (2014)
**Source**: Authentic Education blog
**URL**: https://authenticeducation.org/ubd-and-inquiry-a-response-to-2-questions/

Wiggins responded to two Twitter questions about the relationship between UbD and inquiry-based learning / PBL. A teacher asked whether UbD is compatible with open-ended student-driven inquiry, and whether teacher-designed essential questions undermine student autonomy.

**Wiggins' response**: "I think it's a false either-or." He acknowledged that UbD CAN be used in a closed/convergent way, but does not HAVE to be. He described how he told his English students: "You need to understand that there are no correct final answers to any question of literary interpretation. But some answers are better than others. One purpose of this course is to understand how both statements can be true at the same time."

On why teachers should frame essential questions:
"Why should the teacher frame courses by essential questions? For the same reason that the soccer coach should design most practices: the framing is a function of expertise and experience. It is my job as teacher to know the vital, penetrating, and interesting questions."

On gradual release:
"By the teacher modeling what good questions are and how interesting work can be guided by good questions, the student is more likely to become a better autonomous questioner in the end."

---

### 12 Ways to Kill Understanding by Design from the Start (2014)
**Source**: Authentic Education blog
**URL**: https://authenticeducation.org/12-ways-to-kill-understanding-by-design-ubd-from-the-start/

Wiggins' most direct response to poor implementation of UbD. The opening word - "Sigh." - captures his frustration. The post is both self-critical and critical of how schools implement the framework.

**Top mistakes he identified:**
1. Fixating on terminology and template boxes instead of the underlying purposes
2. Mandating UbD for ALL planning immediately without sufficient training
3. Treating UbD as a one-year initiative
4. Implementing too many initiatives simultaneously
5. Assuming staff understand the need for UbD
6. One-shot PD with no follow-up
7. Training teachers but not administrators
8. Minimal train-the-trainer programs
9. Stage-by-stage training (Year 1 = Stage 1, Year 2 = Stage 2, etc.)
10. Announcing UbD as the official lesson-plan system (it's not a lesson-plan system)
11. Standardizing all implementation without differentiation
12. Starting with any old unit (instead of starting with units that are currently failing)

**Observation on his tone**: The post is simultaneously frustrated and constructive. He opens with exasperation ("Sigh.") but immediately offers practical alternatives ("INSTEAD:") for each mistake. He treats poor implementation as a solvable design problem, not a cause for abandoning the framework.

---

### Beyond Teacher Egocentrism: Design Thinking (2013)
**Source**: Authentic Education blog

Wiggins argued that teachers' belief that "teaching causes learning" is "too egocentric a formulation." He reframed: "The learning is the center of our world, not the teaching. And until we see that we are in the business of designing and causing learning instead of merely in the business of teaching, we will fail to cause optimal learning."

**Key analogy**: "I know this sounds a bit unromantic. It must sound dry to all of us - me included - who entered teaching to 'lay on hands'... But genuine improvement in student learning depends upon such decentering, just as we had to give up the geocentric theory to advance our success in understanding the universe."

**Observation on tone**: Here Wiggins is at his most philosophical and provocative. He anticipates emotional resistance to his idea (teaching is not the center of learning) and addresses it directly, acknowledging his own attachment to the romantic view of teaching. He uses the Copernican analogy to make his argument feel like an inevitable intellectual advance rather than a criticism of teachers.

## 5. Training Workshops on Unit Planning

### The UbD Template Controversy (2012)
**Source**: Authentic Education blog

Wiggins openly discussed the UbD template, its evolution (4 major revisions over 14 years), and the problems caused by rigid implementation. He shared links to alternative templates and encouraged flexibility.

**Key insight**: "The use of planning tools should involve some freedom, based not only on personal planning style and preferences but track record as a teacher-planner. On the other hand, allowing teachers to plan any old way just doesn't work out well. The middle ground is to ensure that key design questions are considered in any specific planner."

---

### Working with Understandings (2011)
**Source**: Authentic Education blog

Wiggins directly addressed the "teacher misunderstanding" that "we have to teach the understanding for understanding to occur." He argued that understanding must be "seen" by learners, must be an "insight" that they earn.

**Key metaphor**: "If you doubt me, just switch gears and think of parenting. How often have you had a child 'learn' the understanding you 'taught' the first or even the fourth time about, say, peer pressure, time management or wise use of allowance money?"

**Practical guidance for workshops**:
- Focus on 1-4 understandings per unit
- Don't always post understandings early - let students discover them
- Use Socratic Seminars and Problem-Based Learning approaches
- "You have to slow down the teaching to speed up the learning."

---

### Autonomy and the Need to Back Off by Design as Teachers (2013)
**Source**: Authentic Education blog

Wiggins argued that teachers over-scaffold and under-challenge students' autonomy. "Everywhere I go I see way too much scaffolded and prompted teaching - through twelfth grade."

**Key coaching metaphor**: "The beauty of soccer coaching (unlike most other sports) is that as a coach you cannot call time out and you cannot script behavior. The sport demands from the start that you coach so as to signal that autonomy in playing winning soccer is the goal."

**Mantra for practice design**: Pro soccer coaches taught him that "every practice must go through cycles of the following: game-related, game-like, game."

**On teacher instincts**: "I understand, this is difficult. It's counter-intuitive to say: please teach less and help less, in order that performance might become more successful over time. Our instincts as teachers cause us to over-help rather than under-help."

## 6. Collaboration with Jay McTighe

### McTighe's Reflections on Wiggins
**Source**: "Three Lessons for Teachers from Grant Wiggins" (PDF, 2018)

Jay McTighe wrote extensively about their partnership after Wiggins' death. He called Wiggins "my brother as well as an intellectual partner and best friend."

**Three lessons McTighe highlighted from Wiggins:**
1. **Plan backward from authentic performance** - "Grant knew that students need to understand 'big ideas' in order to transfer their learning."
2. **Use feedback to improve learning and performance** - Wiggins cautioned that feedback is not grades (B+) or exhortations ("try harder"). Effective feedback is timely, specific, descriptive, and user-friendly.
3. **Develop empathy for students** - Wiggins' "shadow a student" suggestion led to one of the most widely-read education blog posts ever (over 1 million views).

**On Wiggins' "Expert Blind Spot" concept**: "Expressed in the language of the six facets, experts frequently find it difficult to have empathy for the novice, even when they try. That's why teaching is hard, especially for the expert in the field who is a novice teacher."

---

### ASCD Blog: "Back-to-School Lessons from an Education Giant" (2021)
**Author**: Jay McTighe

McTighe wrote: "Grant stressed that a backward-designed curriculum means more than simply determining all of the content and standards you plan to cover and mapping out your day-to-day lessons. The idea is to plan backward from worthy goals - transferable concepts, principles, processes, and essential questions that will enable students to apply their learning in meaningful and authentic ways."

McTighe noted that the six facets of understanding apply to teachers too: "Grant pointed out that the facets have value beyond their use as a frame for curriculum and assessment design. They can be applied to teachers and teaching as well."

---

### How the Collaboration Worked
Wiggins and McTighe had complementary strengths. Wiggins was the assessment expert, the provocative thinker, the writer who "wrote like an unstoppable engine" (Education Week). McTighe brought experience as director of the Maryland Assessment Consortium and deep knowledge of standards-based reform. They met in the early 1990s and their partnership lasted until Wiggins' death in 2015.

McTighe said about Wiggins: "Although I am an only child, I considered Grant my brother as well as an intellectual partner and best friend. I think of Grant every day and still miss him greatly."

## 9. Patterns and Observations Across All Sources

### Communication Style Summary
Wiggins' speaking style can be characterized as:
1. **Provocative but constructive**: He deliberately started with challenging statements ("Teachers spend too much time teaching") but always followed with practical alternatives
2. **Analogically rich**: He constantly used soccer coaching, parenting, video games, and athletics to make abstract educational ideas concrete
3. **Research-grounded**: He cited Tyler (1938), Bransford/Brown/Cocking, Perkins & Salomon, Black & Wiliam - he never argued from ideology alone
4. **Self-aware about limitations**: He openly admitted his own teaching failures (the soccer coaching epiphany, his early assumption about reading comprehension)
5. **Willing to be vulnerable**: He shared stories about his own misunderstandings and growth as a teacher
6. **Comfortable with disagreement**: He engaged skeptics seriously, not defensively
7. **Exceptionally generous**: He answered every email, engaged with critics, mentored young teachers, and built community

### How He Handled Specific Criticisms

**"UbD is too rigid / template-focused"**:
- Acknowledged the problem, took partial responsibility ("a mistake we made was to present one template instead of two or three")
- Distanced himself from rigid implementations by "mandate-minded and unimaginative supervisors"
- Emphasized the principles over the boxes
- Offered variations and alternatives

**"We don't have time for all this assessment"**:
- Reframed: teaching is not more important than learning
- Used the pithy "If you say you don't have time for assessment, you assume that teaching is more important than learning"
- Used the coaching analogy: coaches always make time for feedback
- Argued that "losing time" to assessment actually "gains time" in effectiveness

**"UbD is incompatible with inquiry/PBL/student-centered learning"**:
- Called it "a false either-or"
- Showed how UbD can be open or closed depending on design choices
- Emphasized gradual release of responsibility
- Argued that teacher-framed questions don't preclude student-generated questions

**"This works for the example but not for my context"**:
- Blunt response: "It's your job to make it happen. That's what it means to have a design constraint."
- Reframed the objection as a "design challenge" to be solved
- Did not accept excuses disguised as contextual differences

### The Coaching Metaphor as His Signature
Wiggins returned to sports coaching (especially soccer) more than any other analogy. It served multiple purposes:
1. Made abstract assessment concepts concrete (formative = practice games, summative = championship)
2. Positioned teachers as coaches rather than lecturers - a more active, feedback-oriented role
3. Provided a familiar reference point that most educators could relate to
4. Normalized the idea that performance requires practice, feedback, and multiple attempts
5. Undercut the "no time" objection by showing that coaches always make time for what matters most

The soccer coaching epiphany - where a player yelled "I can't SEE it now; they won't line up like the way we did the drill!" - was his most-repeated story. He used it to illustrate the gap between isolated skill drills and authentic performance, and to argue that transfer must be deliberately taught and assessed.
