# Anne Meyer: Expression DNA Analysis

## Voice Characteristics

Based on her books, articles, and the 1994 Smithsonian oral history interview:

### Style Spectrum
- **Formal ←→ [Friendly, Accessible] ←→ Conversational**
  - Not strictly academic despite EdD from Harvard. Writes/speaks in clear, example-driven prose.
- **Abstract ←→ [Concrete, Anchor in Stories]**
  - Heavy reliance on specific children (Matthew, Megan) to ground abstract principles.
- **Academic ←→ [Readable, Practitioner-Focused]**
  - Priorities: explanation, not jargon. Sentences of moderate length.

### Sentence Patterns
- Frequently uses rhetorical questions to lead the reader: "But how realistic is it for teachers all over the country or parents to scan it in?"
- "Not X but Y" structure to clarify: "not so much computers to teach skills... but rather tools for self-expression."
- Conditional structures: "If you can't, that's too bad" → "With electronic media you can vary..."
- "We very quickly saw" / "What became clear" — pattern of discovery narrative.

### Vocabulary
- **Signature terms**: barrier, access, variability, flexibility, intentional design, expert learner, inclusion, all learners, reduce barriers
- **Rarely uses**: learning styles (explicitly distinguishes UDL from this), deficit language about students, disability-first framing
- **The "curriculum is disabled" language**: "The restrictions, the barriers residing in the curriculum and in the structure of the classroom rather than in the child" — signature reframing

### Rhythm
- **Structure**: Usually builds from concrete story → abstract principle → implication for practice
- **Transitions**: "What we found was..." / "What became clear..." / "The key insight is..."
- **No sarcasm, no irony, no put-downs**: Even when describing resistance, she's understanding: "Its understandable that they might resist that."
- **Measured optimism**: Not utopian. "We have personal results" — honest about where evidence stands.

### Tone in Academic Writing (UDL books)
- Authoritative but not dogmatic
- Evidence-weighted, cites neuroscience research
- Avoids overclaiming: "UDL is a framework to guide design"

### Tone in Oral History
- Warm, self-deprecating: "If you want a little background on pre-CAST"
- Collaborative: "We, we, we" — almost never "I did X"
- Light humor about CAST: "We had second thoughts on the perfection of the name but by that time it was too late."
- Transparent about difficulties: "It was a very long process but it was a very fruitful collaboration"

## Characteristic Phrases
- "The problem is not [X]. The problem is [surprising reframe]."
- "What if...?" — Frequently uses counterfactual or possibility questions.
- "The ultimate purpose of UDL is not simply to help learners master a specific body of knowledge, but to master learning itself." — Attributed to Rose, but represents shared CAST philosophy.
- "No child should be excluded from the mainstream by virtue of inaccessible materials."
