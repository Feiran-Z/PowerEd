# Colin Beard: Conversations & Spontaneous Thinking

## Key Sources

### 1. High 5 Adventure Learning Center Podcast (Feb 2025)
**Title:** "The History of Experiential Learning"
**Host:** Phil Brown
**URL:** https://high5adventure.org/podcast/the-history-of-experiential-learning-professor-colin-beard/

**Key conversational moments:**
- Opens with: "Education wasn't for certain people" — frames experiential learning as an equity issue
- Discusses "prehistory" of learning — human learning capacities evolved over millions of years
- Argues traditional educational models overlook essential cognitive functions
- Champions Maria Montessori and Marina Ewald as "silenced voices" in education history
- Introduces the Holistic Experiential Learning Model (HELM) as a response to oversimplified cycles
- Emphasis on questioning as paramount in the learning process
- Frame: the complexity of learning requires a holistic approach, simplicity in models can be limiting

**Spontaneous thinking patterns:**
- Historical framing first: starts with deep time (millions of years of human learning) before addressing modern education
- Equity lens: connects experiential learning history to who was excluded from formal education
- Uses "silenced voices" framing — systemic bias in whose contributions are remembered

### 2. The Learn/Work Environment as a Managerial Discipline (Nov 2021)
**Podcast with:** René Stevens, ATELIER V
**URL:** https://www.atelier-v.com/ep017-the-learn-work-environment-as-a-managerial-discipline/

**Key quote:**
> "If you are a fish the last thing you would discover is the water. In higher education, we have not given sufficient thought to our surroundings of teaching & learning, even though it is fundamental to the learning process. We have to wake up and realise that we are no longer fish, where we have never thought about the water, or in our case, the places where we teach and learn."

**Definition of Experiential Learning offered spontaneously:**
1. When the experience takes centre stage
2. The design of the experience takes centre stage in the learning (experience-centred)
3. Focused on the learner (learner-centred)
4. Experiential learning is "acquaintance with" rather than "knowledge of"

**Other key points:**
- Introduces "Giraffe Effect" — engagement with the subject from a different perspective
- "Let the learners do the learning. Don't go too fast and don't try to do too much."
- Gesture-based technologies (spatial recognition) have potential to "liberate the human mind to the next layer"
- Argues teachers should have voices heard in facilities/IT/HR discussions about learning spaces
- The environment (physical, digital, social) is a catalyst for people's performance and has strategic impact

**Spontaneous thinking patterns:**
- Uses animal/environment metaphors naturally (fish/water, giraffe effect)
- Direct, practical advice mixed with theoretical framing
- Shifts easily between micro (teacher's voice in meetings) and macro (strategic organisational impact)

## Conversational DNA

### How he responds under questioning:
- **Historical contextualiser:** When asked about experiential learning today, he starts with "humans have been learning throughout their entire existence" (deep time frame)
- **Concrete-first teacher:** Gives direct definitions (the 4-point definition of experiential learning) before elaborating theoretically
- **Analogical thinker:** Natural metaphors (fish/water, giraffe effect, lightbulb/LED) come spontaneously
- **Corrector of misconceptions:** Actively pushes back against what experiential learning is NOT (especially the cycle model)
- **Self-aware practitioner:** Positions himself as still learning, still experimenting
- **Global perspective:** Frequently references work across Asia, Europe, US, South America — not UK-centric

### Distinguishing patterns:
- Uses physical, bodily, spatial metaphors more than abstract ones
- When asked to define, gives 4 precise bullet points (not a paragraph)
- Readily names people he disagrees with or finds limited (Kolb implicitly through HELM, cycle models explicitly)
- Expresses through example and activity, not just principle
- His writing voice on blog is warmer, more personal, less formal than his academic writing

### What he doesn't do verbally:
- Avoids aggressive academic criticism (implicitly critiques through building alternatives)
- Doesn't claim his models are definitive — "selected core modes" (language of provisionality)
- Doesn't position himself as a guru — consistently a "practitioner" and "facilitator" first
